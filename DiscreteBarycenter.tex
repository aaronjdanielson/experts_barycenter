\section{Introduction}

Combining probabilistic forecasts from multiple experts is a central problem in macroeconomics, finance, and public policy.  The Survey of Professional Forecasters (SPF) elicits probability distributions over future GDP growth, inflation, and unemployment from panels of professional economists every quarter; these individual histogram forecasts must be summarized into a single aggregate distribution before informing decisions.  Expert elicitation in public health, climate science, and risk analysis faces the same aggregation problem.  In all of these settings the data consist of probability vectors over a finite outcome space (histogram bins, policy categories, or categorical scenarios), and the goal is to produce a single representative probability distribution.

The dominant method is the \emph{linear opinion pool}, the arithmetic mean of the individual probability vectors \cite{genest86,ohagan06,timmermann06}.  Its appeal is axiomatic: it is the only combination rule consistent with marginalisation across subsets of outcomes \cite{genest86}.  In well-behaved panels with exchangeable, well-calibrated forecasters it is also asymptotically efficient.  Its practical weakness is sensitivity to heterogeneous or extreme opinions.  A single forecaster who places substantial mass on an extreme outcome shifts the aggregate by $1/N$ of that distance, regardless of how far outside the consensus view that forecaster sits.  In the SPF, some forecasters routinely assign 30--50\% probability to tail scenarios that the median forecaster assigns near zero; the arithmetic mean faithfully imports these extreme probabilities into the aggregate.

The forecast combination literature has proposed a range of alternatives to the arithmetic mean \cite{clemen89,timmermann06}.  The \emph{trimmed mean} removes the most extreme forecasters before averaging; it requires choosing a trim fraction and its extension to $K$-dimensional histogram forecasts is not geometrically principled.  The \emph{logarithmic opinion pool} shrinks extreme probabilities by taking the geometric mean, renormalized \cite{genest86}; in our evaluation it achieves the highest probability mass on the realized bin but the worst Ranked Probability Score (RPS)---a divergence we explain analytically via a geometric mismatch between the log pool's mode-seeking objective and the squared-CDF structure of RPS.  Recalibration methods \cite{ranjan10,gneiting13} address a distinct objective: restoring calibration that the linear pool destroys even when individual forecasters are calibrated.  Our objective is forecast accuracy, and we neither claim calibration for the barycenter nor optimize for it.  Combination methods that minimize expected proper scoring-rule loss \cite{gneiting07} require historical accuracy distributions for individual forecasters; our approach requires no track record.  Our contribution is a parameter-free, geometry-based aggregation rule whose properties are analytically characterizable for finite categorical outcome spaces---the setting of histogram forecasts and expert elicitations---and whose regime-conditional advantages are theoretically predicted rather than observed post hoc.

\subsection*{Relation to existing work}

\textit{Trimmed mean.}  The trimmed mean removes the $\lfloor\alpha N\rfloor$ most extreme forecasters at each tail and averages the remainder \cite{lahiri15}.  In our US SPF evaluation it achieves similar RPS to the barycenter (the gap is statistically insignificant by both Wilcoxon $p = 0.82$ and DM $p = 0.63$ tests), but it has two limitations our approach avoids.  First, $\alpha$ must be chosen; the choice is consequential and there is no principled default for $K$-dimensional histogram forecasts, where ``extreme'' is not well-defined without a scalar ordering.  Second, trimming provides no geometric interpretation of why certain opinions are downweighted or when the downweighting helps.  The barycenter is parameter-free and its regime-conditional advantage is predictable from the closed-form theory (Corollary~\ref{cor:local_const}).

\textit{Logarithmic opinion pool.}  The log pool concentrates mass near the geometric mean of the individual densities, which is mode-seeking and aggressive: near-zero probabilities collapse toward zero \cite{genest86}, and extremizing \cite{satopaa14} amplifies this further.  In our evaluation the LP achieves the highest probability on the realized bin but the worst RPS---a divergence we explain analytically via the $\ell^1$--$\ell^2$ mismatch of Remark~\ref{rem:rps_l2}: mode-seeking reduces $\ell^1$ error at the modal bin but increases squared CDF deviations in quarters where a non-modal bin is realized.

\textit{Recalibration.}  \cite{ranjan10} show the linear pool miscalibrates even perfectly calibrated individual forecasts, and \cite{gneiting13} study conditions for recovering calibration.  Our objective is forecast accuracy (RPS), not calibration.  The reliability diagrams in Figure~\ref{fig:reliability} confirm all four methods are approximately calibrated on SPF data; the operative dimension of comparison is where the aggregate concentrates mass, not whether it is globally well-calibrated.

\textit{Scoring-rule-based combination.}  Forecast combination that minimises expected proper scoring-rule loss (see e.g.\ \cite{gneiting07}) requires estimated accuracy weights for individual forecasters and is most natural when historical panel data are available.  The Wasserstein barycenter requires no track record and imposes no model on the distribution of expert opinions, making it applicable to one-shot elicitations, new panels, and rare-event settings where accuracy estimation is infeasible.

\textit{Barycenter-based combination.}  For distributions on $\mathbb{R}$, the $W_2$ barycenter equals the quantile-mean of individual quantile functions \cite{agueh2011barycenters}; \cite{lecoz23} apply this to ensemble forecasting in climatology.  \cite{taylor26} proposes depth-based median aggregates for CDF forecasts.  Our setting---finite categorical outcome spaces---enables a closed-form theory unavailable in the continuous case: the $\ell^1$ simplification (Theorem~\ref{thm:l1}) and the CDF-median formula (Proposition~\ref{prop:w1}) provide an analytically tractable characterization that does not require a solver and yields sharp predictions about the regimes in which each method is preferred.  Reweighting by past accuracy \cite{clemen89,timmermann06,wang23} is complementary: our approach and track-record reweighting could be combined in a weighted barycenter, but we study the equal-weight case to isolate the contribution of geometry.

This paper makes three contributions.

\paragraph{Theory.}  We characterize the Wasserstein barycenter as a parameter-free, geometry-based aggregation rule for finite categorical outcome spaces---the setting of histogram forecasts, policy surveys, and categorical expert elicitations.  Our analysis is specific to this finite setting: the main theoretical result (Theorem~\ref{thm:l1}) shows that the Wasserstein objective over finite unordered spaces simplifies to a constrained $\ell^1$ problem in the probability masses, a simplification without analogue in the continuous setting that makes computation tractable and robustness analysis explicit.  For ordered outcome spaces, the $W_1$ barycenter has a fully closed form: the distribution whose CDF is the component-wise median of the individual CDFs (Proposition~\ref{prop:w1}), computable without a solver.  We prove that the CDF-median barycenter is nonexpansive under panel perturbations and locally constant when the panel median is separated from neighboring CDFs by a positive margin; these properties directly predict the empirical regime structure.  We also identify a geometric mismatch between the barycenter's $\ell^1$ CDF objective and the $\ell^2$-penalizing Ranked Probability Score, which organises the entire empirical evaluation: it predicts when the barycenter wins (low dispersion, consensus close to realized outcome), when it loses (high dispersion, tail realization), and why the log pool---despite being even more aggressive---has the worst RPS.

\paragraph{Empirical evaluation.}  We validate the framework across three independent datasets that differ along every relevant dimension: forecast format (binary vs.\ probability histogram), domain (geopolitical forecasting vs.\ macroeconomics), region (United States vs.\ Euro area), and institution (public tournament vs.\ professional central-bank survey).  We benchmark against the three canonical alternatives: the arithmetic mean (AM), the logarithmic opinion pool (LP), and the symmetrically trimmed arithmetic mean (TM).  (i)~On 94 binary questions from the Good Judgment Project ($\sim\!900$ forecasters each), the $\ell^1$ barycenter (which reduces to the component-wise median for binary outcomes) reduces mean Brier score by 12.5\% relative to the arithmetic mean ($p < 0.001$).  (ii)~On 76 quarters of U.S.\ Survey of Professional Forecasters core CPI histograms (2007--2025), the $W_1$ barycenter and the trimmed mean both achieve lower mean RPS than the AM, while the LP has the worst RPS despite the highest P(correct bin), a divergence explained by the geometric mismatch between the $\ell^1$ objective and $\ell^2$-penalizing RPS; the barycenter's advantage is concentrated in low-dispersion quarters and is significant by both Wilcoxon ($p = 0.005$) and Diebold-Mariano ($p < 0.001$) tests in that regime.  (iii)~A replication on the ECB Survey of Professional Forecasters Euro-area HICP histograms confirms the dispersion-conditional pattern on an independent panel (Wilcoxon $p = 0.012$, DM $p = 0.006$ in low-dispersion quarters).  The dispersion-conditional advantage therefore holds across all three datasets, two continents, and two forecast formats.  A secondary opioid-survey application illustrates the unordered case with $K=20$ outcomes \cite{mcandrew21}.

\paragraph{Adaptive blending.}  We propose a dispersion-adaptive convex blend of the barycenter and the arithmetic mean as a distinct practical contribution.  The blend weight $\lambda_t$ is estimated from an expanding historical window using only the panel's past forecast record---no external data, no expert labeling, and no manually chosen tuning parameter.  The blend is theoretically motivated: Corollary~\ref{cor:local_const} identifies low cross-sectional dispersion as the regime in which the barycenter's local-constancy property activates, and the estimated $\lambda_t$ tracks this regime boundary automatically without a binary dispersion switch.  Empirically, the adaptive blend achieves the lowest out-of-sample RPS of any method evaluated, improving over the trimmed mean by 7.1\%, over the pure barycenter by 1.7\%, and over the arithmetic mean by 4.5\%.  The mixture weight converges to a stable value within the first 20 quarters of the expanding window, indicating the blend is usable early in a panel's life.  We view the blend as a contribution independent of the barycenter: it shows that the theoretical regime structure can be operationalized as a data-driven estimator that strictly outperforms any fixed combination rule.

\section{Wasserstein Barycenters as Aggregate Measures}
\label{sec:wbary}
Let $\Omega = \left\{1,\ldots,K\right\}$ be a finite set of outcomes.  Write $P \in \mathbb{R}^{N\times K}$ for the matrix of expert probability vectors, with row $P_{j\cdot} \in \Delta_{K-1}$ representing expert $j$'s distribution, and let $D$ be the $K\times K$ matrix of ground distances between outcomes.  Because $\Delta_{K-1}$ is compact and the Wasserstein objective is continuous, a barycenter exists for any ground cost \cite{agueh2011barycenters}.  The main result of this section is that when $D$ is the indicator distance, the barycenter objective simplifies to an $\ell^1$ problem in the probability masses (Theorem~\ref{thm:l1}), giving a computationally tractable and geometrically transparent characterization.  The ordered case ($d(x,y)=|x-y|$) yields a further closed-form result (Proposition~\ref{prop:w1}).


\subsection{Definition of the Wasserstein Barycenter}
The following definition can be found in \cite{cuturi}.  Let $(\Omega,d)$ be a metric space for which every measure is a Radon measure and let $\mathcal{P}(\omega)$ be the set of measures on $\Omega$ with finite $p^{\text{th}}$ moment for some element in $\Omega$.
\begin{definition}
A Wasserstein barycenter of $N$ measures $\left\{P_1,\ldots,P_N\right\}$ is a minimizer of
\begin{align*}
f(\Psi)=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^NW^p_p(\Psi,P_i).
\end{align*}
over the set of all measures over the state space $\Omega$ where $W_p$ is the Wasserstein distance given by
\begin{align*}
W_p=\left(\inf_{\pi\in\Gamma(\mu,\nu)}\int d(x,y)^pd\pi(x,y)\right)^{1/p}.
\end{align*}
\end{definition}
The authors of \cite{dbary} present results for the support of discrete Wasserstein barycenter for finitely supported measures.  That is, each measure places mass on a finite number of points in Euclidean space.  The authors show the set of support points is not contained in the union of the support of the individual measures.   Hence discrete Wasserstein barycenters in Euclidean space are not bounded by the component probability measures.  But, we are concerned with the case such that $\Omega=\left\{1,\ldots,K\right\}$.  The definition becomes any $\Psi$ that minimizes
\begin{align*}
\sum_{j=1}^N\left(\min_{\pi\in\Gamma(p_j,\Psi)}\sum_{x=1}^K\sum_{y=1}^K d(x,y)^p\pi(x,y)\right).
\end{align*}
We consider two ground costs: the indicator distance for unordered outcome spaces (Section~\ref{sec:unordered}) and the absolute-value distance $d(x,y)=|x-y|$ for ordered spaces.

\subsection{Unordered Finite Outcome Space}
\label{sec:unordered}
When the distance function is given by
\begin{align*}
d(x,y)= \left\{
\begin{array}{ll}
      1 & x\neq y \\
      0 & x=y \\
\end{array} 
\right.
\end{align*}
choose matrices $\Pi^1,\ldots,\Pi^N$ to minimize the objective function
\begin{align*}
f(\mathbf{\Pi})&=\sum_{l=1}^N\left(\sum_{k=1}^K\sum_{k'=1}^K d(k,k')^2\Pi^l_{k,k'}\right) \\
&=\sum_{l=1}^N\sum_{k=1}^K\sum_{k'\neq k}\Pi^l_{k,k'}
\end{align*}
subject to the feasibility conditions for $j=1,\ldots,N$ and $l,m=1,\ldots,N$
\begin{align*}
&\sum_j\Pi^l_{k,j}=p_l(k) \\
&\sum_j\Pi^l_{j,k}=\sum_j\Pi^m_{j,k}.
\end{align*}
The key identity needed for the main theorem is the following.

\begin{lemma}[Indicator-cost identity]
\label{lem:ot_diagonal}
For $p, \Psi \in \Delta_{K-1}$,
\[
  W_{\mathrm{ind}}(p,\Psi) \;=\; 1 - \sum_k \min\{p(k),\Psi(k)\} \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\|p - \Psi\|_1.
\]
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
The transport cost under the indicator distance is $1 - \sum_k \Pi_{kk}$ for any coupling $\Pi \in \Gamma(p,\Psi)$, minimized by maximising $\sum_k \Pi_{kk}$.  Marginal constraints give $\Pi_{kk} \leq \min\{p(k),\Psi(k)\}$, and the plan $\Pi^*_{kk} = \min\{p(k),\Psi(k)\}$ is feasible (residual row and column masses are nonneg\-ative and sum to the same total, so an off-diagonal assignment exists).  Thus $W_{\mathrm{ind}}(p,\Psi) = 1 - \sum_k \min\{p(k),\Psi(k)\}$.  The second equality uses $a - \min(a,b) = \max(a-b,0)$ and $\sum_k(p(k)-\Psi(k)) = 0$ to give $\sum_k\max\{p(k)-\Psi(k),0\} = \tfrac{1}{2}\|p-\Psi\|_1$.
\end{proof}

The analogous property for barycenter solutions (that every optimal plan simultaneously satisfies $\Pi^{l}_{kk} = \min\{p_l(k), \Psi(k)\}$) is established in Appendix~\ref{app:fulldiag}.
\begin{theorem}[$\ell^1$ Simplification]
\label{thm:l1}
Let $\left\{p_1,\ldots,p_N\right\}$ be probability mass functions on $\left\{1,\ldots,K\right\}$ and let the ground cost be the indicator distance.  Then the Wasserstein barycenter $\Psi^*$ solves
\begin{align}
\label{eq:l1obj}
\min_{\Psi \in \Delta_{K-1}} \sum_{l=1}^N \sum_{k=1}^K \left| p_l(k) - \Psi(k) \right|,
\end{align}
the $\ell^1$ sum of componentwise deviations from the experts.  In particular, a Wasserstein barycenter exists and the Wasserstein objective is equivalent to an $\ell^1$ regression of $\Psi$ onto the expert opinions.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Fix any candidate $\Psi \in \Delta_{K-1}$.  By Lemma~\ref{lem:ot_diagonal}, the optimal transport cost $\mathrm{OT}(\Psi, p_l)$ between $\Psi$ and each $p_l$ under the indicator distance equals
\begin{align*}
\mathrm{OT}(\Psi, p_l) = 1 - \sum_k \min\{p_l(k), \Psi(k)\}
= \tfrac{1}{2}\sum_k |p_l(k) - \Psi(k)|,
\end{align*}
using the identity $a - \min(a,b) = \max(a-b,0)$ and the fact that $\sum_k(p_l(k)-\Psi(k))=0$.  Summing over $l$ gives the objective~\eqref{eq:l1obj} up to a positive constant.  The function $\Psi \mapsto \sum_{l,k}|p_l(k)-\Psi(k)|$ is continuous on the compact simplex, so the minimum is attained.
\end{proof}
\begin{remark}
The identity $\mathrm{OT}(p,\Psi) = \tfrac{1}{2}\|p-\Psi\|_1$ is the standard equivalence between the Wasserstein-1 distance under the indicator cost and the total variation distance; see \cite{panaretos19} for a survey.  The theorem reduces the barycenter problem to $\ell^1$ median estimation on the simplex: the unconstrained minimizer assigns each state the coordinatewise median of the expert opinions (subject to the normalization constraint $\sum_k\Psi(k)=1$), and the simplex projection shifts this toward the feasible median.  The arithmetic mean generally does not satisfy this condition and therefore does not generally coincide with the Wasserstein barycenter.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[Boundedness]
\label{cor:bounded}
Every Wasserstein barycenter $\Psi^*$ under the indicator cost satisfies
\[
  \min_{j} p_j(k) \;\leq\; \Psi^*(k) \;\leq\; \max_{j} p_j(k)
  \qquad \text{for each } k = 1,\ldots,K.
\]
In particular, $\Psi^*$ assigns zero probability to any outcome that no expert assigns positive probability.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
By the Full-Diagonal Criterion (Proposition~\ref{prop:fulldiag}), every optimal transport plan satisfies $\Pi^l_{kk} = \min\{p_l(k), \Psi^*(k)\}$ for all $l$ and $k$.  Suppose $\Psi^*(k) > \max_j p_j(k)$ for some $k$.  Then $\min\{p_l(k), \Psi^*(k)\} = p_l(k)$ for every $l$, so the diagonal entries in column $k$ account for only $\sum_l p_l(k) < N\Psi^*(k)$ units of the column mass $N\Psi^*(k)$.  The remaining mass $N\Psi^*(k) - \sum_l p_l(k) > 0$ must come from off-diagonal entries in column $k$, each incurring transport cost $1$.  Reducing $\Psi^*(k)$ by any $\varepsilon > 0$ small enough (and increasing some other coordinate to maintain the simplex constraint) would eliminate some of this off-diagonal mass, strictly decreasing the objective.  This contradicts optimality, so $\Psi^*(k) \leq \max_j p_j(k)$.  The lower bound $\Psi^*(k) \geq \min_j p_j(k)$ follows symmetrically by considering row masses.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Unanimity]
If all experts agree ($p_1 = p_2 = \cdots = p_N = p$), then $\Psi^* = p$.  This follows immediately from Corollary~\ref{cor:bounded}: each bound collapses to $p(k)$, so $\Psi^*(k) = p(k)$ for every $k$.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[Binary case]
\label{cor:binary}
For $K=2$, the Wasserstein barycenter under the indicator cost is
\[
  \Psi^*(1) \;=\; \mathrm{median}\{p_1(1),\ldots,p_N(1)\}, \qquad \Psi^*(2) = 1 - \Psi^*(1).
\]
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
With $K=2$ the simplex constraint $\Psi(1)+\Psi(2)=1$ reduces the optimisation to a single free variable.  The objective $\sum_l(|p_l(1)-\Psi(1)|+|p_l(2)-\Psi(2)|) = 2\sum_l|p_l(1)-\Psi(1)|$ is minimised by the median of $\{p_l(1)\}$, which automatically lies in $[0,1]$ and hence satisfies the simplex constraint.
\end{proof}
\begin{remark}[Breakdown point]
The finite-sample replacement breakdown point of the binary barycenter is $(\lfloor N/2\rfloor + 1)/N \approx 50\%$, matching the classical sample median: one must corrupt strictly more than half the panel to drive $\Psi^*(1)$ to an arbitrary value.  Any minority of corrupted opinions---however extreme, leaves $\Psi^*(1)$ unchanged provided the corrupted values do not cross the median rank.  This is a precise statement of robustness: the barycenter is locally constant (Corollary~\ref{cor:local_const} gives the exact margin) and globally stable under minority corruption.  By contrast, the arithmetic mean has breakdown point $1/N$: a single extreme opinion shifts the aggregate, and its influence grows without bound as $P_0 \to \delta_k$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Discrete Barycenters for Ordered Spaces}

When the outcome space $\Omega = \left\{1,\ldots,K\right\}$ carries a natural ordering, the indicator distance used in the preceding section discards structural information.  Setting $d(x,y)=|x-y|$ makes $(\Omega,d)$ a metric space that respects the ordering and gives rise to a distinct class of Wasserstein barycenters.  Let $F_j(k)=\sum_{x\leq k}P_j(x)$ denote the cumulative distribution function (CDF) of $P_j$ and let $F_j^{-1}(t)=\inf\left\{k:F_j(k)\geq t\right\}$ denote its quantile function.

\subsubsection*{Wasserstein-1 Barycenters}

For $p=1$, the Wasserstein distance between $P$ and $Q$ on $\Omega$ reduces to the $L^1$ distance between their CDFs \cite{vallender73,panaretos19}:
\begin{align*}
W_1(P,Q)=\sum_{k=1}^{K-1}\left|F_P(k)-F_Q(k)\right|.
\end{align*}
The barycenter minimizes $\sum_{j=1}^N W_1(\Psi,P_j)$ over probability mass functions $\Psi$ on $\Omega$.  Substituting the CDF representation and exchanging the order of summation gives
\begin{align*}
\sum_{j=1}^N W_1(\Psi,P_j)=\sum_{k=1}^{K-1}\sum_{j=1}^N\left|F_\Psi(k)-F_j(k)\right|,
\end{align*}
which separates across $k$.  Each term is independently minimized by the sample median of $\left\{F_j(k)\right\}_{j=1}^N$.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:w1}
Let $\left\{P_1,\ldots,P_N\right\}$ be probability mass functions on $\left\{1,\ldots,K\right\}$ and let $W_1$ be defined with $d(x,y)=|x-y|$.  A Wasserstein-1 barycenter has CDF
\begin{align*}
F_\Psi(k)=\mathrm{med}\left\{F_1(k),\ldots,F_N(k)\right\},\quad k=1,\ldots,K-1.
\end{align*}
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Since the objective separates across $k$, the unconstrained minimizer at each $k$ is the sample median of $\left\{F_j(k)\right\}_{j=1}^N$.  It remains to verify that the vector of component-wise medians constitutes a valid CDF, i.e., it is nondecreasing in $k$.  Since each $F_j$ is nondecreasing, $F_j(k)\leq F_j(k+1)$ for every $j$.  If $x_j\leq y_j$ for all $j$, then $\mathrm{med}\left\{x_j\right\}\leq\mathrm{med}\left\{y_j\right\}$; hence $F_\Psi(k)\leq F_\Psi(k+1)$ for all $k$.  The boundary conditions $F_\Psi(0)=0$ and $F_\Psi(K)=1$ hold since $F_j(0)=0$ and $F_j(K)=1$ for every $j$.  Thus $F_\Psi$ is a valid CDF and $\Psi(k)=F_\Psi(k)-F_\Psi(k-1)\geq 0$ defines a probability mass function on $\Omega$.
\end{proof}

The W-1 barycenter has a natural interpretation: it is the distribution whose CDF lies at the ``center'' of the collection in the $L^1$ sense.

\begin{corollary}[CDF Boundedness]
\label{cor:cdf_bounded}
The $W_1$ barycenter $\Psi^*$ satisfies
\[
  \min_{j} F_j(k) \;\leq\; F_{\Psi^*}(k) \;\leq\; \max_{j} F_j(k)
  \qquad \text{for each } k = 1,\ldots,K-1.
\]
In particular, $\Psi^*$ never assigns cumulative probability outside the range spanned by the panel.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
Immediate from Proposition~\ref{prop:w1}: the component-wise median of $\{F_j(k)\}$ lies in $[\min_j F_j(k),\, \max_j F_j(k)]$.
\end{proof}

\subsubsection*{Robustness of the Ordered Barycenter}

The CDF-median structure of the $W_1$ barycenter implies two stronger robustness results than the perturbation bound of Corollary~\ref{cor:bc_perturb}.

\begin{proposition}[CDF Nonexpansiveness]
\label{prop:nonexpansive}
Let $F^*$ and $\tilde F^*$ denote the $W_1$ barycenter CDFs associated with panels $\{F_1,\ldots,F_N\}$ and $\{\tilde F_1,\ldots,\tilde F_N\}$.  Then
\[
  \|F^* - \tilde F^*\|_\infty \;\leq\; \max_{1\leq j\leq N}\|F_j - \tilde F_j\|_\infty.
\]
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
By Proposition~\ref{prop:w1}, $F^*(k) = \mathrm{med}\{F_j(k)\}$ and $\tilde F^*(k)=\mathrm{med}\{\tilde F_j(k)\}$.  The sample median is $1$-Lipschitz in the $\ell^\infty$ sense: for any real vectors $x,y\in\mathbb{R}^N$, $|\mathrm{med}(x) - \mathrm{med}(y)|\leq\|x-y\|_\infty$.  Applying this coordinatewise gives $|F^*(k)-\tilde F^*(k)|\leq\max_j|F_j(k)-\tilde F_j(k)|\leq\max_j\|F_j-\tilde F_j\|_\infty$.  Taking the supremum over $k$ proves the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Proposition~\ref{prop:nonexpansive} contrasts sharply with the arithmetic mean: $\|\bar F - \tilde{\bar F}\|_\infty \leq \frac{1}{N}\sum_j\|F_j-\tilde F_j\|_\infty$.  The AM bound involves the \emph{average} CDF perturbation, so a single large outlier contributes $1/N$ of its full shift.  The barycenter bound involves the \emph{maximum} perturbation: one very large shift dominates.  This is median-like behavior — but the key practical implication runs in the opposite direction of a naive reading: the barycenter's sup-norm bound is no worse than the largest single-panel perturbation, whereas the AM's bound can be far smaller when outliers are isolated.  What the bound does guarantee is that the barycenter cannot be destabilized by many small perturbations all in the same direction.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[Margin-Based Local Constancy]
\label{cor:local_const}
Assume $N$ is odd.  For each $k = 1,\ldots,K-1$, let $\gamma_k$ be the gap between the median CDF value and its nearest neighboring order statistic:
\[
  \gamma_k = \min\bigl\{F_{(m)}(k) - F_{(m-1)}(k),\; F_{(m+1)}(k) - F_{(m)}(k)\bigr\},
\]
where $F_{(1)}(k)\leq\cdots\leq F_{(N)}(k)$ are the order statistics of $\{F_j(k)\}$ and $m = (N+1)/2$.  If
\[
  \max_{j,\,k}\,|\tilde F_j(k) - F_j(k)| \;<\; \min_k \gamma_k,
\]
then $\tilde F^* = F^*$: the $W_1$ barycenter is completely unchanged by the perturbation.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
Under the stated condition, the perturbation is strictly smaller than the median gap at every coordinate.  Therefore the rank ordering of $\{\tilde F_j(k)\}$ is identical to that of $\{F_j(k)\}$ at each $k$, so the median order statistic is the same element.  Hence $\tilde F^*(k)=F^*(k)$ for all $k$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Corollary~\ref{cor:local_const} formalizes why the barycenter is robust in consensus regimes: when the panel CDFs are tightly clustered (large $\gamma_k$), the aggregate is completely immune to perturbations smaller than the cluster gap.  The arithmetic mean has no analogous immunity: any perturbation, however small, shifts the mean CDF by exactly $1/N$ of that amount.
\end{remark}

\subsubsection*{Connection to the Ranked Probability Score}

The empirical evaluation in Section~\ref{sec:spf_backtest} uses the Ranked Probability Score (RPS) as its primary criterion.  The following remark makes the geometric relationship between the $W_1$ barycenter and RPS explicit, which explains the dispersion-conditional pattern observed in the data.

\begin{remark}[RPS as squared $\ell^2$ CDF distance]
\label{rem:rps_l2}
For an ordered outcome space with $K$ bins, the Ranked Probability Score of a forecast CDF $F_{\hat p}$ against realized bin $b$ is
\[
  \mathrm{RPS}(F_{\hat p},\, b) \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{K-1} \bigl(F_{\hat p}(k) - \mathbf{1}_{b \leq k}\bigr)^2,
\]
the squared $\ell^2$ distance in CDF space between $F_{\hat p}$ and the step CDF of a point mass at bin $b$.  By contrast, Proposition~\ref{prop:w1} shows that the $W_1$ barycenter minimizes an \emph{$\ell^1$} objective in CDF space across the panel.  This $\ell^1$--$\ell^2$ mismatch has a direct empirical implication: the barycenter concentrates its CDF near the panel median, which reduces $\ell^2$ CDF error when the realized outcome falls in the consensus region (low dispersion), but can increase $\ell^2$ CDF error when the realized outcome is a tail bin (high dispersion), because the arithmetic mean's broader CDF is geometrically closer to extreme step functions.  Section~\ref{sec:spf_backtest} documents both patterns.
\end{remark}

\section{Robustness of Aggregating Measures}
\label{sec:robustness}

Both the arithmetic mean and the Wasserstein barycenter are bounded aggregating measures, as established in the preceding sections.  They differ, however, in their sensitivity to extreme or outlying expert opinions.  This section formalizes that difference via the empirical influence function and illustrates it through simulation.

\subsection{Empirical Influence Function}

The empirical influence function (EIF) quantifies the effect of introducing a single additional expert $P_0$ into an existing collection $\mathbf{P}_N = \left\{P_1,\ldots,P_N\right\}$.  For an aggregating measure $T$, the EIF is
\begin{align*}
\mathrm{EIF}(P_0;\, T,\, \mathbf{P}_N) = (N+1)\left[T\!\left(\mathbf{P}_N \cup \left\{P_0\right\}\right) - T\!\left(\mathbf{P}_N\right)\right],
\end{align*}
rescaled so that it approximates the derivative of $T$ with respect to contamination fraction $\varepsilon = 1/(N+1)$.  The gross error sensitivity $\mathrm{GES}(T) = \sup_{P_0} \left\|\mathrm{EIF}(P_0;\,T,\,\mathbf{P}_N)\right\|_\infty$ summarizes the worst-case influence of a single outlier.

\begin{proposition}[\textbf{EIF of the Arithmetic Mean}]
\label{prop:am_eif}
Let $\bar{P}_N = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=1}^N P_j$ be the arithmetic mean of $\mathbf{P}_N$.  Then
\begin{align*}
\mathrm{EIF}_k(P_0;\, \bar{P},\, \mathbf{P}_N) = P_0(k) - \bar{P}_N(k)
\end{align*}
for each state $k \in \Omega$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Adding $P_0$ to $\mathbf{P}_N$ gives $\bar{P}_{N+1}(k) = \frac{N\bar{P}_N(k) + P_0(k)}{N+1}$, so
\[
(N+1)\bigl[\bar{P}_{N+1}(k) - \bar{P}_N(k)\bigr] = P_0(k) - \bar{P}_N(k). \qedhere
\]
\end{proof}
\begin{remark}
The EIF of the arithmetic mean is linear in $P_0$ and independent of $N$: every opinion, regardless of how extreme, exerts unit influence on the aggregate.  The gross error sensitivity is $\mathrm{GES}(\bar{P}) = \sup_{P_0}\|P_0 - \bar{P}_N\|_\infty \leq 1 - \min_k \bar{P}_N(k)$, which approaches $1$ as $P_0 \to \delta_k$ for any $k$.
\end{remark}

The exact EIF of the Wasserstein barycenter does not admit a closed form in general, because the barycenter is defined as the solution to a nonlinear variational problem whose geometry changes when $P_0$ is added.  A useful bound is available, however, from the boundedness result of Corollary~\ref{cor:bounded}.

\begin{corollary}[\textbf{Perturbation Bound for the Wasserstein Barycenter}]
\label{cor:bc_perturb}
Let $\hat\Psi_N$ and $\hat\Psi_{N+1}$ denote the Wasserstein barycenters of $\mathbf{P}_N$ and $\mathbf{P}_N\cup\{P_0\}$ respectively under the indicator cost.  Then for each $k$,
\begin{align*}
\left|\hat\Psi_{N+1}(k) - \hat\Psi_N(k)\right|
\;\leq\; \max_{j\leq N} P_j(k) \vee P_0(k) \;-\; \min_{j\leq N} P_j(k) \wedge P_0(k).
\end{align*}
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
By Corollary~\ref{cor:bounded}, $\hat\Psi_N(k)$ lies in $[\min_{j\leq N}P_j(k),\,\max_{j\leq N}P_j(k)]$ and $\hat\Psi_{N+1}(k)$ lies in the analogous interval for the combined collection.  The difference of two values in these intervals cannot exceed the width of the combined range.
\end{proof}

The two results reveal a structural asymmetry.  The EIF of the arithmetic mean equals $P_0 - \bar{P}_N$ exactly, independent of $N$: every expert exerts unit influence on the aggregate regardless of how extreme their opinion is.  The barycenter's shift is bounded by the cross-sectional range of expert opinions (Corollary~\ref{cor:bc_perturb}), constraining the maximum impact of any single outlier.  This does not imply uniformly smaller sensitivity in finite samples---in particular, when the panel is homogeneous and no expert is an outlier, the barycenter and AM respond similarly---but it does mean that a minority of extreme opinions cannot move the barycenter beyond the range already spanned by the panel.  Stronger robustness results for the ordered case (nonexpansiveness and local constancy under median margins) are established in Section~\ref{sec:wbary}.

\subsection{Simulation Evidence}

To illustrate the practical difference, we simulate $R = 200$ collections of $N = 11$ experts.  Ten experts are drawn from a uniform Dirichlet distribution over $K = 6$ states.  One expert is drawn from a concentrated Dirichlet placing substantial mass on a single state (the ``outlier'').  For each collection, both the arithmetic mean and the Wasserstein barycenter are computed.

Figure~\ref{fig:outlier_boxplot} displays the distribution of the probability assigned to the outlier's preferred state ($S_6$) across the $R$ replicates.  The arithmetic mean is substantially more inflated: in $90\%$ of replicates the AM assigns more probability to $S_6$ than the barycenter does, consistent with its equal-weight treatment of the outlier.  Figure~\ref{fig:variance} shows the variance of each aggregate across replicates by state.  The barycenter has lower variance on the outlier state ($0.0039$ vs.\ $0.0016$ for the AM on $S_6$) but higher overall variance, confirming the classical robustness--efficiency tradeoff.

Figure~\ref{fig:single_draw} displays a single representative draw: five regular experts, one outlier, and the two resulting aggregates.  The arithmetic mean is visibly pulled toward the outlier's distribution; the barycenter remains closer to the consensus of the regular experts.

Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity} varies the number of outlying experts from zero to three.  The arithmetic mean's assignment to $S_6$ grows approximately linearly with outlier count.  The barycenter grows more slowly, reflecting its distributional resistance.

\begin{remark}
The higher variance of the barycenter across replicates is expected.  When expert opinions are drawn from a well-specified population, the arithmetic mean is the minimum-variance unbiased estimator of the population mean.  The barycenter trades efficiency for robustness, analogous to the median relative to the mean in location estimation.  The practical choice between the two depends on the degree of belief in the homogeneity of the expert pool.
\end{remark}

\section{Simulations}
\label{sec:simulations}

The robustness analysis of the preceding section held the structure of contamination fixed: one outlying expert drawn from a concentrated Dirichlet, $N-1$ regular experts drawn from a uniform Dirichlet.  This section broadens the comparison through a systematic simulation study examining (1) efficiency under a correctly specified population model and (2) robustness under varying degrees of misspecification.

\subsection{Setup}

All simulations use $K=6$ states.  Expert opinions are drawn from one of two Dirichlet populations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Regular experts}: $P_j \sim \mathrm{Dir}(\mathbf{1}_K)$, the uniform Dirichlet placing equal prior weight on all states.
    \item \textbf{Outlying experts}: $P_j \sim \mathrm{Dir}(\alpha_{\mathrm{out}})$ with $\alpha_{\mathrm{out}} = (1,1,1,1,1.5,10)^{\odot 4}$, a highly concentrated distribution placing substantial mass on state $S_6$.
\end{itemize}
For each simulation, $N_{\mathrm{reg}}$ regular and $N_{\mathrm{out}}$ outlying opinions are drawn; the arithmetic mean and Wasserstein barycenter (with uniform weights and indicator distance) are computed; and the results are summarized across $R = 200$ replicates.  Performance is measured by (i) mean squared error relative to the population mean of the regular experts, (ii) variance across replicates, and (iii) probability assigned to the outlier's preferred state $S_6$.

\subsection{Efficiency Under the Null}

When all experts are drawn from the same regular population ($N_{\mathrm{out}} = 0$), the arithmetic mean is the minimum-variance unbiased estimator of the population probability vector.  Table~\ref{tab:efficiency} compares the mean squared error of the two aggregates relative to the true population mean $\mu = \mathbf{1}_K / K$ for varying $N$.

\section{Good Judgment Project: Binary Forecasts}
\label{sec:gjp}

We provide a scored binary evaluation using data from the Good Judgment Project (GJP), a geopolitical forecasting tournament conducted under the IARPA ACE program from 2011 to 2015~\cite{tetlock16}.  An unordered illustration with $K=20$ outcomes (opioid-policy expert survey) is provided in Appendix~\ref{app:opioid}.

For binary outcomes, Corollary~\ref{cor:binary} gives that the indicator-cost $\ell^1$ barycenter reduces to the component-wise median of the individual probability vectors.  Each forecaster's distribution over \{Yes, No\} is characterized by a single number $p_j \in [0,1]$; the barycenter selects $p^* = \mathrm{median}_j(p_j)$ while the arithmetic mean selects $\bar{p} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_j p_j$.  The dispersion-robustness intuition is direct: if a minority of forecasters assigns extreme probabilities near 0 or 1, the median is unaffected while the mean is pulled toward them.

\paragraph{Data and protocol.}  We use individual survey forecasts from Year~1 of the GJP (2011--2012), publicly available through Harvard Dataverse~\cite{tetlock_dataverse}.  For each of the 94 binary questions with at least 5 participating forecasters (mean panel size: 895), we take each forecaster's last submitted forecast before question closure and compute the arithmetic mean and median of the individual $P(\text{Yes})$ values.  We score each aggregate against the binary resolution using the Brier score, $\mathrm{BS} = (p - \mathbf{1}_{\text{Yes}})^2$.

\paragraph{Results.}  The median (barycenter) achieves a lower Brier score than the arithmetic mean on 85.1\% of questions, with mean Brier scores of 0.091 (BC) versus 0.104 (AM), a 12.5\% improvement ($p < 0.001$, Wilcoxon).  The advantage is present across all dispersion terciles and is largest when the panel exhibits low disagreement (Table~\ref{tab:gjp} and Figure~\ref{fig:gjp_brier}).

\section{Application to the Survey of Professional Forecasters}
\label{sec:spf}

The opioid survey (Appendix~\ref{app:opioid}) treats the 20 interventions as an \emph{unordered} outcome space, so the indicator distance is the natural choice.  Many expert-aggregation problems, however, involve \emph{ordered} outcomes: survey respondents assign probabilities to ordered bins.  For such data the $W_1$ barycenter has the closed-form characterization derived in Proposition~\ref{prop:w1}: it equals the component-wise median of the empirical CDFs.  This section illustrates both the practical convenience of that formula and its robustness implications using data from the Survey of Professional Forecasters (SPF).

\subsection{Data}

The Philadelphia Federal Reserve Bank has administered the SPF every quarter since 1968.  In each survey, professional forecasters submit probabilistic predictions for macroeconomic variables by allocating probability mass across a fixed set of ordered bins.  We use the \emph{core CPI} probability question (variable \texttt{PRCCPI}), which asks each forecaster to assign probabilities to 10 ordered bins for fourth-quarter-over-fourth-quarter core CPI inflation in the current and following year.  The 10 bins are (in ascending order): decline, 0.0--0.4\%, 0.5--0.9\%, 1.0--1.4\%, 1.5--1.9\%, 2.0--2.4\%, 2.5--2.9\%, 3.0--3.4\%, 3.5--3.9\%, and $\geq 4.0\%$.

We focus on the 2022:Q1 survey's \emph{next-year} histograms, each forecaster's probability distribution for 2023 core CPI inflation, using the $n=27$ respondents who provided a complete histogram summing to 100\%.  This survey was conducted in February 2022, when realized core CPI was running near 6\% and forecasters disagreed sharply about how quickly inflation would moderate.

\subsection{Aggregate Distributions}

Figure~\ref{fig:spf_heatmap} displays the 27 individual histograms as a heatmap.  The heterogeneity is striking: some forecasters concentrated nearly all mass on 2--3\% (expecting a rapid return to target), while others spread mass across higher bins.  A few placed non-trivial probability on the decline and $\geq 4\%$ tail bins.

\centering

\caption{SPF 2022:Q1: probability distributions for 2023 core CPI inflation across 27 professional forecasters.  Each row is one forecaster's histogram over 10 ordered bins (ascending from ``will decline'' to $\geq 4\%$).  Cell color encodes probability mass (darker = higher).}
\label{fig:spf_heatmap}

Because the outcomes are ordered, we use the $W_1$ distance (absolute rank difference) as the ground cost.  This respects the ordinal structure of the inflation bins: shifting probability from the 2--3\% bin to the 3--4\% bin incurs less transport cost than shifting it to the deflation bin, correctly reflecting the asymmetry in the outcomes.  By Proposition~\ref{prop:w1}, the $W_1$ barycenter equals the PMF obtained by component-wise CDF median.  We verified this numerically: the closed-form formula and the LP solver agree to machine precision ($\ell^1$ discrepancy $< 10^{-4}$).

Figure~\ref{fig:spf_aggregates} compares the arithmetic mean and the $W_1$ barycenter.  The differences are substantial.  The arithmetic mean places 3.6\% mass on the ``decline'' bin and 8.1\% on the $\geq 4\%$ bin, for a combined tail probability of 11.7\%.  The barycenter assigns \emph{zero} mass to both tails.  Instead, it concentrates 60\% of its mass on the 2.0--2.9\% range and 30\% on 3.0--3.9\%, reflecting the view shared by a majority of forecasters.  The arithmetic mean disperses mass across all bins because a minority of extreme views pull it toward the tails.

\subsection{Historical Forecast Evaluation}
\label{sec:spf_backtest}

\subsubsection*{Evaluation protocol}

\noindent\textbf{Forecast variable.}  We use the current-year PRCCPI histograms (columns \texttt{PRCCPI1}--\texttt{PRCCPI10}), in which each respondent distributes 100 probability points across 10 ordered bins for Q4/Q4 core CPI inflation in the current calendar year.  The horizon is current-year (not next-year); we choose this horizon because the realized value is unambiguously assigned within the same calendar year and the panel is largest.

\noindent\textbf{Sample construction.}  The sample covers all survey quarters from 2007:Q1 through 2025:Q4.  A respondent's row is retained if its bin probabilities sum to within 2 percentage points of 100; retained histograms are renormalized to exactly 1 and reversed to ascending bin order.  Quarters with fewer than five valid respondents are excluded, leaving $n = 76$ usable quarters.

\noindent\textbf{Realized outcomes.}  Each realized value is the December-over-December percent change in seasonally adjusted core CPI (FRED series \texttt{CPILFESL}) for the forecast year.  The SA and NSA series are nearly identical for this variable.  Each realized value is mapped to one of the 10 bins using the boundaries in Table~\ref{tab:backtest}.

\noindent\textbf{Scoring rules.}  The primary scoring rule is the Ranked Probability Score (RPS; see Remark~\ref{rem:rps_l2})~\cite{gneiting07}: lower RPS is better.  The secondary criterion is the probability mass the aggregate places on the realized bin (mass score); higher is better.  While not a proper scoring rule, the mass score provides an interpretable measure of concentration at the realized outcome and complements RPS, which penalizes dispersion across the full CDF.  The two criteria together distinguish methods that concentrate near the correct outcome from those that spread probability broadly.

\noindent\textbf{Inference.}  Score differences are tested via two complementary procedures.  One-sided Wilcoxon signed-rank tests~\cite{gneiting07} assess whether a method's RPS is stochastically smaller than the AM's (robust to non-normality and heavy tails).  Diebold-Mariano (DM) tests~\cite{diebold95} assess whether the mean loss differential is zero, using Newey-West HAC standard errors to account for serial dependence.  The two tests are complementary: Wilcoxon measures \emph{consistency} of the advantage (win rate), while DM measures its \emph{average magnitude}.  When the loss differential is skewed---as in 2021--2022, where both methods suffered large losses---the tests can diverge, and we report both.

\noindent\textbf{Calibration.}  We assess calibration via bin-level reliability diagrams: for each of the 10 bins and each method, we plot the mean predicted probability against the empirical frequency of that bin being realized.  Well-calibrated forecasts lie on the 45-degree line.  Results are reported in Figure~\ref{fig:reliability}.

The single-quarter illustration above motivates a question of practical importance: does the $W_1$ barycenter produce better probabilistic forecasts than the arithmetic mean when evaluated against realized outcomes?

\paragraph{Overall results.}  We compare four methods: the arithmetic mean (AM), the logarithmic opinion pool (LP), the symmetrically trimmed arithmetic mean (TM, 10\% trim), and the $W_1$ barycenter (BC).  Panel A of Table~\ref{tab:backtest} reports both metrics over the full 76-quarter sample.  The TM achieves the lowest mean RPS among the four methods ($0.435$, wins 76.3\% vs.\ AM, Wilcoxon $p < 0.001$, DM $p = 0.002$), with the BC close behind ($0.439$, wins 68.4\%, Wilcoxon $p = 0.005$).  The Wilcoxon and DM tests tell a complementary story for BC vs.\ AM: Wilcoxon confirms BC wins more often, while DM $p = 0.225$ indicates the \emph{average magnitude} of the gain is not individually significant---reflecting the fact that 2021--2022 inflation-surge quarters inflated the variance of the loss differential and attenuated the mean gain.  The dispersion-conditional DM tests resolve this: in low-dispersion quarters both tests agree strongly (DM $p < 0.001$, Wilcoxon $p < 0.001$), confirming the BC's structural advantage precisely where the theory predicts it.  The apparent RPS gap between TM and BC ($0.435$ vs.\ $0.439$) is not statistically significant by either test (Wilcoxon $p = 0.82$, DM $p = 0.63$): TM does not reliably outperform the BC; its narrow average advantage disappears in the adaptive blend.  The LP has the worst RPS ($0.486$, DM $p = 0.95$) despite the highest P(correct bin) ($0.491$ vs.\ $0.424$ for BC).  This divergence (LP is best at placing mass on the realized bin yet worst at RPS) reflects the $\ell^1$--$\ell^2$ mismatch of Remark~\ref{rem:rps_l2}: the LP's extreme concentration minimizes $\ell^1$ error at the mode, but its near-zero tail mass amplifies squared CDF error in the increasingly many quarters where a tail bin is realized.  The BC balances both objectives: it places substantially more mass on the realized bin than the AM (75.0\% win rate, $p < 0.001$) while preserving more tail coverage than the LP.

\paragraph{Dispersion-conditional results.}  The theory makes a sharp prediction about when the barycenter advantage is largest.  Corollary~\ref{cor:local_const} shows the $W_1$ barycenter is locally constant when panel CDFs are separated from their neighbors by a positive margin: low cross-sectional dispersion corresponds to tightly clustered CDFs with large median margins, so the barycenter is most stable and most concentrated on the consensus precisely when the panel broadly agrees.  We therefore test a pre-specified, theory-driven partition: quarters in which the panel agrees (low dispersion) versus quarters in which it is genuinely divided (high dispersion).  Panel B splits the sample at the median cross-sectional variance into 38 low- and 38 high-dispersion quarters.  The probability-on-realized-bin metric shows the barycenter outperforming in \emph{both} groups: 0.496 versus 0.430 in low-dispersion quarters, and 0.353 versus 0.303 in high-dispersion quarters.  The RPS results are more nuanced: the barycenter wins decisively in low-dispersion quarters (mean RPS 0.301 vs.\ 0.328, $p < 0.001$), while the arithmetic mean is marginally better in high-dispersion quarters (mean RPS 0.567 vs.\ 0.578, $p = 0.37$).  The divergence between the two metrics in high-dispersion quarters reflects the $\ell^1$--$\ell^2$ mismatch identified in Remark~\ref{rem:rps_l2}: the barycenter minimizes an $\ell^1$ CDF objective, so it concentrates near the panel median; but RPS penalizes squared CDF deviations, and the AM's broader CDF is geometrically closer to the step CDFs of tail outcomes.  The barycenter still places more mass on the realized bin even in high-dispersion quarters, but the AM's retained tail mass yields smaller cumulative squared deviations when an extreme outcome materializes.  The 2021--2022 inflation surge is the clearest illustration: both methods underestimated realized core CPI ($\geq 4\%$), but the AM retained more tail mass, reducing its RPS deficit in those extreme quarters.

In low-dispersion quarters the economic interpretation is straightforward: the panel agrees, the barycenter correctly concentrates mass on the consensus region, and the consensus is usually right.  In high-dispersion quarters the panel is genuinely uncertain; the arithmetic mean's equal weighting preserves more tail mass, which pays off under RPS when an extreme outcome materializes, even though the barycenter still places more mass on the correct bin in expectation.

\subsection{Adaptive Blending}
\label{sec:blend}

The historical results suggest a natural extension.  The $W_1$ barycenter improves on the arithmetic mean on average, but the arithmetic mean retains value in high-dispersion quarters under the RPS.  A convex blend
\[
  \hat{\Psi}_t(\lambda) \;=\; \lambda\,\Psi^*_t + (1-\lambda)\,\bar{P}_t,\qquad \lambda\in[0,1],
\]
interpolates between the two extremes.  At $\lambda=1$ this is the barycenter; at $\lambda=0$ it is the arithmetic mean.

\paragraph{Oracle.}  A grid search over $\lambda\in\{0,0.005,\ldots,1\}$ using all 76 quarters identifies $\lambda^*=0.70$ as the mean-RPS minimizer, yielding an in-sample mean RPS of $0.437$, lower than both the arithmetic mean ($0.447$) and the pure barycenter ($0.439$).  The improvement is modest but reveals that neither extreme is optimal.

\paragraph{Out-of-sample evaluation.}  To avoid look-ahead bias we apply an expanding-window protocol: for each quarter $t$ (starting from quarter 21) we estimate $\hat\lambda_t$ by minimizing mean RPS over all prior quarters and apply it only to quarter $t$.  In a richer adaptive version, $\hat\lambda_t = \sigma(a_t + b_t D_t)$ where $D_t$ is the cross-sectional variance of individual forecast means and $(a_t, b_t)$ are estimated by Nelder-Mead on the expanding window.  Results over the 56-quarter out-of-sample period are reported in Table~\ref{tab:blend} and Figure~\ref{fig:blend}.

\subsection{Replication on the ECB Survey of Professional Forecasters}
\label{sec:ecb_spf}

To assess whether the US SPF findings are specific to that survey or reflect a more general property of barycenter aggregation, we replicate the backtest on the ECB Survey of Professional Forecasters (ECB SPF), which has been conducted quarterly since 1999~\cite{garcia05}.  The ECB SPF covers the Euro area and asks respondents to assign probabilities to ordered bins for headline HICP inflation, real GDP growth, and the unemployment rate.  We use the headline HICP current-year forecasts over the period 2010Q1--2024Q3 ($n=56$ quarter-surveys), during which the bin structure is stable at 12 ordered bins of width 0.5~pp from below $-1\%$ to above $4\%$.  Realized values are annual-average HICP YoY from the ECB Statistical Data Warehouse.  The evaluation protocol (AM, $W_1$ barycenter, RPS scoring, dispersion split) is identical to Section~\ref{sec:spf_backtest}.

\paragraph{Results.}  The barycenter assigns more probability mass to the realized outcome bin in 78.6\% of quarter-surveys ($p<0.001$, Wilcoxon), with a mean mass of $0.457$ versus $0.430$ for the arithmetic mean, a 6.3\% improvement (Figure~\ref{fig:ecb_mass}).  This replicates the core finding from the US SPF on an independent panel in a different institutional setting and with a different realized variable.

The aggregate RPS advantage is near zero overall (mean RPS $0.441$ AM vs.\ $0.441$ BC, Wilcoxon $p=0.23$), but this masks a clean dispersion-conditional pattern that precisely replicates the US SPF finding.  In low-dispersion quarters ($n=28$), the barycenter achieves meaningfully lower RPS (mean $0.255$ vs.\ $0.266$), and this advantage is statistically significant by both tests (Wilcoxon $p=0.012$, DM $p=0.006$).  In high-dispersion quarters the arithmetic mean wins marginally (mean $0.616$ vs.\ $0.626$), with no significant difference (Wilcoxon $p=0.57$, DM $p=0.89$).  The overall near-tie is explained by the 2021--2023 Euro-area inflation surge: annual-average HICP reached $8.4\%$ in 2022, well above the highest bin boundary (${\geq}4\%$), an outcome that both methods failed to anticipate but that the arithmetic mean handles less badly under RPS by retaining more tail mass.  These extreme quarters dominate the high-dispersion subsample and attenuate the aggregate average.  The replication finding is therefore not a null result but a confirmation: the dispersion-conditional pattern holds on an independent panel with a different variable, a different bin structure, and a different institutional setting.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

This paper proposes the Wasserstein barycenter as a robust method for aggregating expert probability forecasts over finite outcome spaces.  The central theoretical result is Theorem~\ref{thm:l1}: under the indicator ground cost, the barycenter reduces to a constrained $\ell^1$ aggregation problem.  This simplification has two important consequences.  First, it makes computation straightforward: the problem is a convex linear program in $NK$ variables, solvable by standard LP solvers; for ordered spaces, Proposition~\ref{prop:w1} gives a fully closed-form answer (component-wise CDF median) with no solver required.  Second, it identifies the barycenter as a median-type aggregate: the unconstrained minimizer of the $\ell^1$ objective assigns the coordinatewise median to each state, and the simplex constraint shifts this toward a normalized version of the median.  For ordered outcome spaces the closed form is exact: the $W_1$ barycenter is the distribution whose CDF is the component-wise median of the individual CDFs (Proposition~\ref{prop:w1}), a result verified numerically to machine precision in the SPF application.

\paragraph{When the barycenter wins and when it does not.}  The barycenter is not a uniformly superior alternative to the arithmetic mean, and we do not claim it is.  In high-dispersion quarters---when the panel is genuinely divided and tail outcomes are plausible---the arithmetic mean's broader CDF yields lower RPS, because it retains the tail coverage that squared-CDF scoring rewards when an extreme bin is realized.  The trimmed mean achieves similar overall RPS to the barycenter (the gap is not statistically significant by either Wilcoxon or DM tests), and it remains a reasonable choice when a simple, interpretable aggregate is needed.  The barycenter's advantage is concentrated in low-dispersion quarters, where the theoretical regime structure (Corollary~\ref{cor:local_const}) predicts it: tightly clustered CDFs imply large median margins, so the aggregate is locally constant and concentrates mass precisely where the panel agrees.  The adaptive blend is preferred over any fixed method when a calibration window is available, because it does not commit to either regime.

\paragraph{Robustness interpretation.}  The EIF analysis formalizes the intuition that the barycenter is less sensitive than the arithmetic mean to extreme opinions.  The AM gives every expert equal weight regardless of distance from the consensus; the barycenter penalizes outlying opinions through transport cost, so an expert far from the current aggregate exerts smaller influence.  Empirically, in $90\%$ of simulation replicates the barycenter assigns less probability to an outlier's preferred state than the AM.  The tradeoff is efficiency: under a well-specified homogeneous expert population, the AM has lower MSE by a factor of approximately $1.4$--$1.9$.

\paragraph{Forecast evaluation.}  The historical backtest in Section~\ref{sec:spf_backtest} provides direct evidence that the theoretical properties of the barycenter translate into empirical gains.  The barycenter assigns more probability mass to the realized outcome bin in a majority of quarters, with a mean improvement of 15.6\% (0.424 vs.\ 0.367), and achieves a lower average Ranked Probability Score than the arithmetic mean.  The advantage is concentrated in low-dispersion quarters; the arithmetic mean recovers ground under RPS in high-dispersion quarters because its broader tail coverage reduces cumulative CDF deviations when extreme outcomes materialize.  A dispersion-adaptive convex blend of the two methods achieves the lowest out-of-sample RPS ($0.404$), versus $0.411$ for the pure barycenter and $0.423$ for the arithmetic mean.  The optimal mixture weight ($\lambda^*\approx0.70$) is stable over time, matching the oracle value within the expanding-window evaluation.  The dispersion-adaptive blend achieves the lowest overall RPS ($0.404$), outperforming the trimmed mean ($0.435$), pure barycenter ($0.411$), and arithmetic mean ($0.423$) in the out-of-sample evaluation.  These results together characterize when each method is preferred: the barycenter is the better single aggregate when the panel is broadly in agreement, while the arithmetic mean's equal weighting preserves tail mass that is occasionally valuable when extreme outcomes materialize, and the blend dominates both by adapting continuously.  The Wasserstein barycenter is not calibrated by construction: calibration is a property of the individual expert forecasts and is not in general preserved under nonlinear combination.  The reliability diagrams (Figure~\ref{fig:reliability}) confirm no systematic miscalibration across methods; all four aggregates track the 45-degree line without evidence of over- or under-confidence.  The comparison against the logarithmic pool and the trimmed mean (Table~\ref{tab:backtest}) reveals that the $\ell^1$--$\ell^2$ mismatch between the pooling objective and RPS is not specific to the barycenter: the LP, which concentrates mass even more aggressively, achieves the highest P(correct bin) but the worst RPS.  The trimmed mean, which excises extreme forecasters symmetrically, achieves the lowest single-method RPS.  Extending the evaluation to multi-horizon forecasts, other outcome variables, and extremization~\cite{satopaa14} are natural further directions.

\begin{remark}[Practical guidance]
The primary recommendation is the dispersion-adaptive convex blend (Section~\ref{sec:blend}): it requires no decision about which regime applies, achieves the lowest out-of-sample RPS of any method evaluated, and automatically adjusts to the panel's cross-sectional agreement.  When a single non-adaptive aggregate is needed, the choice is guided by dispersion.  When cross-sectional dispersion is low (the panel is broadly in agreement), the $W_1$ barycenter is preferred: it concentrates mass on the consensus region and achieves lower RPS than both AM and LP, with the advantage significant by both Wilcoxon and Diebold-Mariano tests.  When dispersion is high (the panel is genuinely divided and tail outcomes are plausible), the arithmetic mean or trimmed mean preserves the tail coverage needed for strong RPS performance under extreme realizations.  The blend operationalizes this transition continuously rather than as a binary switch.
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Applications and extensions.}  The applications illustrate the method on both ordered (SPF, $W_1$ distance) and unordered (opioid survey, indicator distance; Appendix~\ref{app:opioid}) outcome spaces.  A natural extension is to weighted barycenters, in which experts receive unequal weights reflecting their track record or domain expertise.  A natural population model for expert probability vectors is the Dirichlet distribution; however, aggregation via Dirichlet MLE does not generally satisfy boundedness or unanimity, and therefore differs conceptually from the deterministic aggregation rules studied here.  The extension to probability distributions over network-valued outcomes (Appendix~\ref{app:networks}) illustrates that the framework applies to any finite metric space without modification.  The multivariate case, in which experts provide joint distributions over multiple outcomes, raises new questions about the structure of the barycenter that we leave for future work.

\section{Barycenters for Network-Valued Outcomes}
\label{app:networks}

The framework extends naturally to settings where the outcome space is a set of network structures \cite{danielson18}.  Let $\mathcal{Y}_n$ denote the space of binary adjacency matrices on $n$ nodes: the set of $n\times n$ zero-diagonal matrices with entries in $\left\{0,1\right\}$, equipped with the Hamming distance
\begin{align*}
d(Y,Y')=\sum_{m\neq n}\left|Y_{mn}-Y'_{mn}\right|.
\end{align*}
A probability mass function over $\mathcal{Y}_n$ assigns probability $p_j(Y)$ to each network $Y\in\mathcal{Y}_n$.  Existence of the barycenter follows from the compactness argument of Theorem~\ref{thm:l1} applied to the finite metric space $(\mathcal{Y}_n, d)$.  The full-diagonal criterion (Proposition~\ref{prop:fulldiag}) carries over: in any optimal solution, $\Pi^{(j)}_{YY}=\min\{p_j(Y),\Psi(Y)\}$ for each $Y$.

Unlike the unordered case where all off-diagonal pairs are equidistant, pairs of networks differing in $k$ edges incur cost $k$ under $W_1$ (or $k^2$ under $W_2$).  After diagonal entries are maximized, remaining mass is assigned to off-diagonal entries in order of increasing Hamming distance.

The result extends to integer-valued (weighted) networks by replacing $\{0,1\}$ with $\{0,\ldots,M\}$ and using the $\ell^1$ Hamming distance.

\section{Full-Diagonal Criterion}
\label{app:fulldiag}

The following proposition extends Lemma~\ref{lem:ot_diagonal} from individual transport plans to the full barycenter solution: in any optimal solution, every transport plan simultaneously maximises its diagonal.

\begin{proposition}[Full-Diagonal Criterion]
\label{prop:fulldiag}
Let $\Pi^{1},\ldots,\Pi^{N}$ be $K\times K$ matrices representing the joint distributions between a candidate barycenter $\Psi$ and the probability mass functions $p_1,\ldots,p_N$ on $\Omega=\left\{1,\ldots,K\right\}$.  Suppose the matrices are feasible: for all $l$ and $k$,
\begin{align*}
\sum_{j}\Pi_{k,j}^{l}=p_l(k),\qquad
\sum_{j}\Pi_{j,k}^{l}=\Psi(k),
\end{align*}
but for some $l$ and $i$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Pi_ineq}
\Pi^{l}_{i,i} < \min\bigl\{p_l(i),\,\Psi(i)\bigr\}.
\end{equation}
Then there exists a feasible $\tilde{\Pi}^{l}\neq \Pi^{l}$ such that
$f(\tilde{\Pi}^l,\Pi^{-l}) < f(\Pi^l,\Pi^{-l})$,
so $(\Pi^1,\ldots,\Pi^N)$ is not optimal.  Consequently, every optimal transport plan satisfies $\Pi^{l}_{kk}=\min\{p_l(k),\Psi(k)\}$ for all $k$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
The objective equals $f(\mathbf{\Pi})= \sum_{l=1}^N \sum_{k=1}^K (1 - \Pi_{k,k}^{l})$, so increasing any diagonal entry strictly decreases the objective.  Suppose \eqref{eq:Pi_ineq} holds.  Since $\Pi^l_{ii} < p_l(i)$, there exists $k_1\neq i$ with $\Pi^l_{i,k_1}>0$.  Setting $\tilde\Pi^l_{i,i}=\Pi^l_{i,i}+\Pi^l_{i,k_1}$ and $\tilde\Pi^l_{i,k_1}=0$ preserves row $i$'s sum.  Since $\Pi^l_{ii}<\Psi(i)$, the column-$i$ sum $\Psi(i)$ exceeds the diagonal; there exists a set of indices $\{k^i_2\}$ and weights $\{\epsilon_i\}$ summing to $\Pi^l_{i,k_1}$ such that transferring mass $\epsilon_i\Pi^l_{k^i_2,i}$ from column $i$ to column $k_1$ restores all column sums.  The resulting $\tilde\Pi^l$ is feasible and the objective decreases by $\Pi^l_{i,k_1}>0$.
\end{proof}
