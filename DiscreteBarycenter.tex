\section{Introduction}

Combining probabilistic forecasts from multiple experts is a central problem in macroeconomics, finance, and public policy.  The Survey of Professional Forecasters (SPF) elicits probability distributions over future GDP growth, inflation, and unemployment from panels of professional economists every quarter; these individual histogram forecasts must be summarized into a single aggregate distribution before informing decisions.  Expert elicitation in public health, climate science, and risk analysis faces the same aggregation problem.  In all of these settings the data consist of probability vectors over a finite outcome space (histogram bins, policy categories, or categorical scenarios), and the goal is to produce a single representative probability distribution.

The dominant method is the \emph{linear opinion pool}, the arithmetic mean of the individual probability vectors \cite{genest86,ohagan06,timmermann06}.  Its appeal is axiomatic: it is the only combination rule consistent with marginalisation across subsets of outcomes \cite{genest86}.  In well-behaved panels with exchangeable, well-calibrated forecasters it is also asymptotically efficient.  Its practical weakness is sensitivity to heterogeneous or extreme opinions.  A single forecaster who places substantial mass on an extreme outcome shifts the aggregate by $1/N$ of that distance, regardless of how far outside the consensus view that forecaster sits.  In the SPF, some forecasters routinely assign 30--50\% probability to tail scenarios that the median forecaster assigns near zero; the arithmetic mean faithfully imports these extreme probabilities into the aggregate.

The forecast combination literature has proposed a range of alternatives to the arithmetic mean \cite{clemen89,timmermann06}.  The \emph{logarithmic opinion pool} (geometric mean, renormalized) shrinks extreme probabilities because the geometric mean of a near-zero probability is near zero \cite{genest86}; \cite{satopaa14} show that \emph{extremizing} (multiplying log-odds by a factor greater than one) sharpens aggregate predictions when experts are individually underconfident.  \cite{ranjan10} establish that nontrivially combined calibrated forecasts are in general no longer calibrated under the linear pool, and \cite{gneiting13} study conditions under which combining predictive distributions can recover calibration.  A different robustness strategy is trimming: removing the most extreme forecasters before averaging, analogous to a trimmed mean in location estimation.  Proper scoring rules provide a principled basis for evaluating probabilistic forecasts \cite{gneiting07}, and combination methods that minimize expected scoring-rule loss have been studied in the continuous-distribution setting.  Other nonlinear pooling methods, such as power means, modify the influence of extreme probabilities through functional transformation; our approach instead introduces robustness through the geometry of the outcome space via optimal transport.  None of these approaches offers a geometry-based robustness characterization that is directly interpretable for finite-category histogram forecasts.

\subsection*{Relation to existing work}

The forecast-combination literature offers three main strategies for reducing sensitivity to extreme opinions in probability forecasts.  The first is recalibration: \cite{ranjan10} show that linearly combined calibrated forecasts are generally no longer calibrated, and various nonlinear pools have been proposed to restore calibration \cite{gneiting13}.  The second is trimming: removing the most extreme forecasters before averaging, studied in the SPF context by \cite{lahiri15} and others.  The third is reweighting by past accuracy, the subject of extensive theoretical and empirical work \cite{clemen89,timmermann06,wang23}.  All three strategies are most natural when historical forecaster-level data are available for calibration or track-record estimation; in settings where such data are limited (a new panel, a rare event, a one-shot expert elicitation), a distribution-free approach is preferable.

Barycenter-based combination has recently been studied in the continuous-distribution setting.  For distributions on $\mathbb{R}$, the $W_2$ barycenter equals the distribution whose quantile function is the component-wise mean of individual quantile functions \cite{agueh2011barycenters}; \cite{lecoz23} apply this to multi-model ensemble forecasting in climatology.  \cite{taylor26} proposes depth-based trimmed means and median-like aggregates for interval and CDF forecasts, sharing our goal of robust aggregation but using a different geometric construction.

This paper proposes the \emph{Wasserstein barycenter} as a robust alternative for settings where historical reweighting by forecast accuracy is unavailable or undesirable: one-shot expert elicitations, new panels, or rare events where track records are absent.  The barycenter minimizes total transport cost from the aggregate to the expert distributions; extreme opinions require large transport cost to incorporate and therefore receive less influence on the aggregate.  Our main theoretical contribution is showing that over finite unordered outcome spaces, the Wasserstein objective simplifies to a constrained $\ell^1$ problem in the probability masses (Theorem~\ref{thm:l1}), a simplification without analogue in the continuous setting that makes both computation and robustness analysis tractable.  For ordered outcome spaces, the natural setting for histogram forecasts, the $W_1$ barycenter admits a fully explicit closed form: the distribution whose CDF is the component-wise median of the individual CDFs (Proposition~\ref{prop:w1}), computable without a solver.  We apply this framework to the SPF histogram forecasts and show that the barycenter suppresses tail mass that a minority of extreme forecasters impose on the arithmetic mean, in a setting where trimming and reweighting approaches require data not always available.

Our empirical contribution has two parts.  First, a single-quarter illustration using the 2022:Q1 SPF core CPI histograms shows the qualitative difference between the two aggregation methods: the $W_1$ barycenter assigns zero mass to the tail bins that a minority of extreme forecasters drive into the arithmetic mean, concentrating on the 2--4\% range favored by the majority, and the LP solver and the closed-form formula agree to machine precision.  Second, a historical backtest covering 76 quarters (2007:Q1--2025:Q4) evaluates both methods against realized Q4/Q4 core CPI outcomes using proper scoring rules.  The barycenter achieves a statistically significant improvement in mean Ranked Probability Score (Wilcoxon $p = 0.005$) and places more mass on the realized bin in 75\% of quarters; the advantage is concentrated in low-dispersion quarters and a dispersion-conditional analysis identifies when each method is preferred.  A secondary application to a survey of 30 public health experts allocating a budget across 20 opioid-related interventions illustrates the unordered-outcome case: with the indicator ground cost, the barycenter shifts mass toward majority-preferred interventions and away from highly dispersed supply-side interventions \cite{mcandrew21}.

\section{Wasserstein Barycenters as Aggregate Measures}
\label{sec:wbary}
Let $\Omega = \left\{1,\ldots,K\right\}$ be a finite set of outcomes.  Write $P \in \mathbb{R}^{N\times K}$ for the matrix of expert probability vectors, with row $P_{j\cdot} \in \Delta_{K-1}$ representing expert $j$'s distribution, and let $D$ be the $K\times K$ matrix of ground distances between outcomes.  Because $\Delta_{K-1}$ is compact and the Wasserstein objective is continuous, a barycenter exists for any ground cost \cite{agueh2011barycenters}.  The main result of this section is that when $D$ is the indicator distance, the barycenter objective simplifies to an $\ell^1$ problem in the probability masses (Theorem~\ref{thm:l1}), giving a computationally tractable and geometrically transparent characterization.  The ordered case ($d(x,y)=|x-y|$) yields a further closed-form result (Proposition~\ref{prop:w1}).


\subsection{Definition of the Wasserstein Barycenter}
The following definition can be found in \cite{cuturi}.  Let $(\Omega,d)$ be a metric space for which every measure is a Radon measure and let $\mathcal{P}(\omega)$ be the set of measures on $\Omega$ with finite $p^{\text{th}}$ moment for some element in $\Omega$.
\begin{definition}
A Wasserstein barycenter of $N$ measures $\left\{P_1,\ldots,P_N\right\}$ is a minimizer of
\begin{align*}
f(\Psi)=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^NW^p_p(\Psi,P_i).
\end{align*}
over the set of all measures over the state space $\Omega$ where $W_p$ is the Wasserstein distance given by
\begin{align*}
W_p=\left(\inf_{\pi\in\Gamma(\mu,\nu)}\int d(x,y)^pd\pi(x,y)\right)^{1/p}.
\end{align*}
\end{definition}
The authors of \cite{dbary} present results for the support of discrete Wasserstein barycenter for finitely supported measures.  That is, each measure places mass on a finite number of points in Euclidean space.  The authors show the set of support points is not contained in the union of the support of the individual measures.   Hence discrete Wasserstein barycenters in Euclidean space are not bounded by the component probability measures.  But, we are concerned with the case such that $\Omega=\left\{1,\ldots,K\right\}$.  The definition becomes any $\Psi$ that minimizes
\begin{align*}
\sum_{j=1}^N\left(\min_{\pi\in\Gamma(p_j,\Psi)}\sum_{x=1}^K\sum_{y=1}^K d(x,y)^p\pi(x,y)\right).
\end{align*}
We consider two ground costs: the indicator distance for unordered outcome spaces (Section~\ref{sec:unordered}) and the absolute-value distance $d(x,y)=|x-y|$ for ordered spaces.

\subsection{Unordered Finite Outcome Space}
\label{sec:unordered}
When the distance function is given by
\begin{align*}
d(x,y)= \left\{
\begin{array}{ll}
      1 & x\neq y \\
      0 & x=y \\
\end{array} 
\right.
\end{align*}
choose matrices $\Pi^1,\ldots,\Pi^N$ to minimize the objective function
\begin{align*}
f(\mathbf{\Pi})&=\sum_{l=1}^N\left(\sum_{k=1}^K\sum_{k'=1}^K d(k,k')^2\Pi^l_{k,k'}\right) \\
&=\sum_{l=1}^N\sum_{k=1}^K\sum_{k'\neq k}\Pi^l_{k,k'}
\end{align*}
subject to the feasibility conditions for $j=1,\ldots,N$ and $l,m=1,\ldots,N$
\begin{align*}
&\sum_j\Pi^l_{k,j}=p_l(k) \\
&\sum_j\Pi^l_{j,k}=\sum_j\Pi^m_{j,k}.
\end{align*}
Note that the objective function is the sum of the off-diagonal probabilities,
\begin{align*}
\sum_k\sum_{k'\neq k}\Pi^l(k,k')=1-\sum_k\Pi^l(k,k).
\end{align*}
Hence minimizing the objective function is equivalent to maximizing the probability mass placed on the diagonal entries of the joint probability density matrices $\left\{\Pi^l\right\}$.  The following lemma shows that this maximum is achieved by every optimal transport plan, and identifies the value of the diagonal entries.

\begin{lemma}[Indicator-cost OT]
\label{lem:ot_diagonal}
Let $p, \Psi \in \Delta_{K-1}$.  Under the indicator cost, every optimal transport plan $\Pi^* \in \Gamma(p, \Psi)$ satisfies
\begin{align*}
\Pi^*_{kk} = \min\bigl\{p(k),\, \Psi(k)\bigr\} \quad \text{for all } k.
\end{align*}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Since $d(k,k')=0$ when $k=k'$ and $d(k,k')=1$ otherwise, the transport cost equals $1 - \sum_k \Pi_{kk}$, so any optimal plan maximises $\sum_k \Pi_{kk}$.  For any feasible $\Pi \in \Gamma(p,\Psi)$ the marginal constraints give $\sum_{k'}\Pi_{kk'} = p(k)$ and $\sum_{k'}\Pi_{k'k} = \Psi(k)$, so $\Pi_{kk} \leq \min\{p(k), \Psi(k)\}$.  The plan $\Pi^*_{kk} = \min\{p(k),\Psi(k)\}$ attains the upper bound.  Feasibility requires the residual row mass $p(k)-\Pi^*_{kk}=\max\{p(k)-\Psi(k),0\}$ and residual column mass $\Psi(k)-\Pi^*_{kk}=\max\{\Psi(k)-p(k),0\}$ to be assigned to off-diagonal entries.  Both residuals are nonneg\-ative and their totals across $k$ are equal (both equal $\sum_k\max\{p(k)-\Psi(k),0\}$, since $p$ and $\Psi$ are probability vectors), so a feasible assignment of the residuals to off-diagonal entries exists by a standard flow argument.
\end{proof}

The analogous property for barycenter solutions is established in Appendix~\ref{app:fulldiag}: every optimal plan $\Pi^{l}$ simultaneously satisfies $\Pi^{l}_{kk} = \min\{p_l(k), \Psi(k)\}$.
\begin{theorem}[$\ell^1$ Simplification]
\label{thm:l1}
Let $\left\{p_1,\ldots,p_N\right\}$ be probability mass functions on $\left\{1,\ldots,K\right\}$ and let the ground cost be the indicator distance.  Then the Wasserstein barycenter $\Psi^*$ solves
\begin{align}
\label{eq:l1obj}
\min_{\Psi \in \Delta_{K-1}} \sum_{l=1}^N \sum_{k=1}^K \left| p_l(k) - \Psi(k) \right|,
\end{align}
the $\ell^1$ sum of componentwise deviations from the experts.  In particular, a Wasserstein barycenter exists and the Wasserstein objective is equivalent to an $\ell^1$ regression of $\Psi$ onto the expert opinions.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Fix any candidate $\Psi \in \Delta_{K-1}$.  By Lemma~\ref{lem:ot_diagonal}, the optimal transport cost $\mathrm{OT}(\Psi, p_l)$ between $\Psi$ and each $p_l$ under the indicator distance equals
\begin{align*}
\mathrm{OT}(\Psi, p_l) = 1 - \sum_k \min\{p_l(k), \Psi(k)\}
= \tfrac{1}{2}\sum_k |p_l(k) - \Psi(k)|,
\end{align*}
using the identity $a - \min(a,b) = \max(a-b,0)$ and the fact that $\sum_k(p_l(k)-\Psi(k))=0$.  Summing over $l$ gives the objective~\eqref{eq:l1obj} up to a positive constant.  The function $\Psi \mapsto \sum_{l,k}|p_l(k)-\Psi(k)|$ is continuous on the compact simplex, so the minimum is attained.
\end{proof}
\begin{remark}
The identity $\mathrm{OT}(p,\Psi) = \tfrac{1}{2}\|p-\Psi\|_1$ is the standard equivalence between the Wasserstein-1 distance under the indicator cost and the total variation distance; see \cite{panaretos19} for a survey.  The theorem reduces the barycenter problem to $\ell^1$ median estimation on the simplex: the unconstrained minimizer assigns each state the coordinatewise median of the expert opinions, and the simplex constraint shifts this toward a normalized version of the median.  The arithmetic mean generally does not satisfy this condition and therefore does not generally coincide with the Wasserstein barycenter.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Discrete Barycenters for Ordered Spaces}

When the outcome space $\Omega = \left\{1,\ldots,K\right\}$ carries a natural ordering, the indicator distance used in the preceding section discards structural information.  Setting $d(x,y)=|x-y|$ makes $(\Omega,d)$ a metric space that respects the ordering and gives rise to a distinct class of Wasserstein barycenters.  Let $F_j(k)=\sum_{x\leq k}P_j(x)$ denote the cumulative distribution function (CDF) of $P_j$ and let $F_j^{-1}(t)=\inf\left\{k:F_j(k)\geq t\right\}$ denote its quantile function.

\subsubsection*{Wasserstein-1 Barycenters}

For $p=1$, the Wasserstein distance between $P$ and $Q$ on $\Omega$ reduces to the $L^1$ distance between their CDFs \cite{vallender73,panaretos19}:
\begin{align*}
W_1(P,Q)=\sum_{k=1}^{K-1}\left|F_P(k)-F_Q(k)\right|.
\end{align*}
The barycenter minimizes $\sum_{j=1}^N W_1(\Psi,P_j)$ over probability mass functions $\Psi$ on $\Omega$.  Substituting the CDF representation and exchanging the order of summation gives
\begin{align*}
\sum_{j=1}^N W_1(\Psi,P_j)=\sum_{k=1}^{K-1}\sum_{j=1}^N\left|F_\Psi(k)-F_j(k)\right|,
\end{align*}
which separates across $k$.  Each term is independently minimized by the sample median of $\left\{F_j(k)\right\}_{j=1}^N$.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:w1}
Let $\left\{P_1,\ldots,P_N\right\}$ be probability mass functions on $\left\{1,\ldots,K\right\}$ and let $W_1$ be defined with $d(x,y)=|x-y|$.  A Wasserstein-1 barycenter has CDF
\begin{align*}
F_\Psi(k)=\mathrm{med}\left\{F_1(k),\ldots,F_N(k)\right\},\quad k=1,\ldots,K-1.
\end{align*}
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Since the objective separates across $k$, the unconstrained minimizer at each $k$ is the sample median of $\left\{F_j(k)\right\}_{j=1}^N$.  It remains to verify that the vector of component-wise medians constitutes a valid CDF, i.e., it is nondecreasing in $k$.  Since each $F_j$ is nondecreasing, $F_j(k)\leq F_j(k+1)$ for every $j$.  If $x_j\leq y_j$ for all $j$, then $\mathrm{med}\left\{x_j\right\}\leq\mathrm{med}\left\{y_j\right\}$; hence $F_\Psi(k)\leq F_\Psi(k+1)$ for all $k$.  The boundary conditions $F_\Psi(0)=0$ and $F_\Psi(K)=1$ hold since $F_j(0)=0$ and $F_j(K)=1$ for every $j$.  Thus $F_\Psi$ is a valid CDF and $\Psi(k)=F_\Psi(k)-F_\Psi(k-1)\geq 0$ defines a probability mass function on $\Omega$.
\end{proof}

The W-1 barycenter has a natural interpretation: it is the distribution whose CDF lies at the ``center'' of the collection in the $L^1$ sense.  Boundedness of $\Psi$ in the CDF sense is immediate since the median is bounded by the minimum and maximum: $\min_j F_j(k)\leq F_\Psi(k)\leq\max_j F_j(k)$ for all $k$.

\section{Robustness of Aggregating Measures}
\label{sec:robustness}

Both the arithmetic mean and the Wasserstein barycenter are bounded aggregating measures, as established in the preceding sections.  They differ, however, in their sensitivity to extreme or outlying expert opinions.  This section formalizes that difference via the empirical influence function and illustrates it through simulation.

\subsection{Empirical Influence Function}

The empirical influence function (EIF) quantifies the effect of introducing a single additional expert $P_0$ into an existing collection $\mathbf{P}_N = \left\{P_1,\ldots,P_N\right\}$.  For an aggregating measure $T$, the EIF is
\begin{align*}
\mathrm{EIF}(P_0;\, T,\, \mathbf{P}_N) = (N+1)\left[T\!\left(\mathbf{P}_N \cup \left\{P_0\right\}\right) - T\!\left(\mathbf{P}_N\right)\right],
\end{align*}
rescaled so that it approximates the derivative of $T$ with respect to contamination fraction $\varepsilon = 1/(N+1)$.  The gross error sensitivity $\mathrm{GES}(T) = \sup_{P_0} \left\|\mathrm{EIF}(P_0;\,T,\,\mathbf{P}_N)\right\|_\infty$ summarizes the worst-case influence of a single outlier.

\begin{proposition}[\textbf{EIF of the Arithmetic Mean}]
Let $\bar{P}_N = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=1}^N P_j$ be the arithmetic mean of $\mathbf{P}_N$.  Then
\begin{align*}
\mathrm{EIF}_k(P_0;\, \bar{P},\, \mathbf{P}_N) = P_0(k) - \bar{P}_N(k)
\end{align*}
for each state $k \in \Omega$.  The gross error sensitivity is $\mathrm{GES}(\bar{P}) = \sup_{P_0}\left\|P_0 - \bar{P}_N\right\|_\infty$, which is maximized as $P_0 \to \delta_k$ for any $k$, giving $\mathrm{GES}(\bar{P}) \leq 1 - \min_k \bar{P}_N(k)$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Adding $P_0$ to $\mathbf{P}_N$ gives $\bar{P}_{N+1}(k) = \frac{N\bar{P}_N(k) + P_0(k)}{N+1}$.  Then
\begin{align*}
(N+1)\left[\bar{P}_{N+1}(k) - \bar{P}_N(k)\right]
= (N+1)\cdot\frac{P_0(k) - \bar{P}_N(k)}{N+1}
= P_0(k) - \bar{P}_N(k).
\end{align*}
The EIF is linear in $P_0$ and equals zero only when $P_0 = \bar{P}_N$.  As $P_0(k)\to 1$ for any $k$, the influence approaches $1 - \bar{P}_N(k)$, which is bounded away from zero whenever $K > 1$.
\end{proof}

For the Wasserstein barycenter, the EIF depends on the geometry of the Wasserstein space.  Adding $P_0$ introduces a new term $\frac{1}{N+1}W_p^p(\Psi, P_0)$ into the barycenter objective, which must be balanced against the $\frac{1}{N+1}$ reduction in weight on each of the $N$ existing terms.  The new minimizer $\hat\Psi_{N+1}$ shifts from $\hat\Psi_N$ only to the extent that moving toward $P_0$ does not excessively increase the total Wasserstein cost relative to the existing $N$ experts.

\begin{proposition}[\textbf{EIF of the Wasserstein Barycenter}]
Let $\hat\Psi_N$ and $\hat\Psi_{N+1}$ denote the Wasserstein barycenters of $\mathbf{P}_N$ and $\mathbf{P}_N\cup\left\{P_0\right\}$ respectively.  The shift $\hat\Psi_{N+1}(k) - \hat\Psi_N(k)$ is bounded by the range of expert opinions on state $k$:
\begin{align*}
\left|\hat\Psi_{N+1}(k) - \hat\Psi_N(k)\right|
\leq \max\!\left(\max_{j\leq N} P_j(k),\, P_0(k)\right) - \min\!\left(\min_{j\leq N} P_j(k),\, P_0(k)\right).
\end{align*}
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Both $\hat\Psi_N(k)$ and $\hat\Psi_{N+1}(k)$ lie within the range of their respective expert collections (Theorem~\ref{thm:l1}, boundedness), so their difference cannot exceed the range of the combined collection.
\end{proof}

The two propositions reveal a structural asymmetry.  The EIF of the arithmetic mean equals $P_0 - \bar{P}_N$ exactly, independent of $N$: every expert, regardless of how extreme, exerts unit influence on the aggregate.  The barycenter's shift is bounded by the range of expert opinions; an outlying opinion far from the existing consensus is costly in the Wasserstein sense and therefore exerts reduced influence.  This is median-like in spirit: the barycenter's response is constrained by the geometry of the existing expert panel rather than being a linear function of all views.  We do not claim a full robust-statistics treatment with sharp breakdown-point results; rather, the bound provides an interpretable upper limit on how much any single opinion can shift the aggregate.

\subsection{Simulation Evidence}

To illustrate the practical difference, we simulate $R = 200$ collections of $N = 11$ experts.  Ten experts are drawn from a uniform Dirichlet distribution over $K = 6$ states.  One expert is drawn from a concentrated Dirichlet placing substantial mass on a single state (the ``outlier'').  For each collection, both the arithmetic mean and the Wasserstein barycenter are computed.

Figure~\ref{fig:outlier_boxplot} displays the distribution of the probability assigned to the outlier's preferred state ($S_6$) across the $R$ replicates.  The arithmetic mean is substantially more inflated: in $90\%$ of replicates the AM assigns more probability to $S_6$ than the barycenter does, consistent with its equal-weight treatment of the outlier.  Figure~\ref{fig:variance} shows the variance of each aggregate across replicates by state.  The barycenter has lower variance on the outlier state ($0.0039$ vs.\ $0.0016$ for the AM on $S_6$) but higher overall variance, confirming the classical robustness--efficiency tradeoff.

Figure~\ref{fig:single_draw} displays a single representative draw: five regular experts, one outlier, and the two resulting aggregates.  The arithmetic mean is visibly pulled toward the outlier's distribution; the barycenter remains closer to the consensus of the regular experts.

Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity} varies the number of outlying experts from zero to three.  The arithmetic mean's assignment to $S_6$ grows approximately linearly with outlier count.  The barycenter grows more slowly, reflecting its distributional resistance.

\begin{remark}
The higher variance of the barycenter across replicates is expected.  When expert opinions are drawn from a well-specified population, the arithmetic mean is the minimum-variance unbiased estimator of the population mean.  The barycenter trades efficiency for robustness, analogous to the median relative to the mean in location estimation.  The practical choice between the two depends on the degree of belief in the homogeneity of the expert pool.
\end{remark}

\section{Simulations}
\label{sec:simulations}

The robustness analysis of the preceding section held the structure of contamination fixed: one outlying expert drawn from a concentrated Dirichlet, $N-1$ regular experts drawn from a uniform Dirichlet.  This section broadens the comparison through a systematic simulation study examining (1) efficiency under a correctly specified population model and (2) robustness under varying degrees of misspecification.

\subsection{Setup}

All simulations use $K=6$ states.  Expert opinions are drawn from one of two Dirichlet populations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Regular experts}: $P_j \sim \mathrm{Dir}(\mathbf{1}_K)$, the uniform Dirichlet placing equal prior weight on all states.
    \item \textbf{Outlying experts}: $P_j \sim \mathrm{Dir}(\alpha_{\mathrm{out}})$ with $\alpha_{\mathrm{out}} = (1,1,1,1,1.5,10)^{\odot 4}$, a highly concentrated distribution placing substantial mass on state $S_6$.
\end{itemize}
For each simulation, $N_{\mathrm{reg}}$ regular and $N_{\mathrm{out}}$ outlying opinions are drawn; the arithmetic mean and Wasserstein barycenter (with uniform weights and indicator distance) are computed; and the results are summarized across $R = 200$ replicates.  Performance is measured by (i) mean squared error relative to the population mean of the regular experts, (ii) variance across replicates, and (iii) probability assigned to the outlier's preferred state $S_6$.

\subsection{Efficiency Under the Null}

When all experts are drawn from the same regular population ($N_{\mathrm{out}} = 0$), the arithmetic mean is the minimum-variance unbiased estimator of the population probability vector.  Table~\ref{tab:efficiency} compares the mean squared error of the two aggregates relative to the true population mean $\mu = \mathbf{1}_K / K$ for varying $N$.

Both estimators are consistent: MSE decreases at rate $O(1/N)$.  The barycenter's efficiency loss relative to the AM grows with $N$, reaching a ratio of $1.86$ at $N=20$.  This reflects the $L^1$ vs.\ $L^2$ character of the two estimators: as more data arrives, the AM concentrates its distribution more tightly, while the barycenter's Wasserstein objective introduces a systematic dispersion penalty.

\subsection{Robustness Under Contamination}

\section{Application to the Survey of Professional Forecasters}
\label{sec:spf}

The opioid survey treats the 20 interventions as an \emph{unordered} outcome space, so the indicator distance is the natural choice.  Many expert-aggregation problems, however, involve \emph{ordered} outcomes: survey respondents assign probabilities to ordered bins.  For such data the $W_1$ barycenter has the closed-form characterization derived in Proposition~\ref{prop:w1}: it equals the component-wise median of the empirical CDFs.  This section illustrates both the practical convenience of that formula and its robustness implications using data from the Survey of Professional Forecasters (SPF).

\subsection{Data}

The Philadelphia Federal Reserve Bank has administered the SPF every quarter since 1968.  In each survey, professional forecasters submit probabilistic predictions for macroeconomic variables by allocating probability mass across a fixed set of ordered bins.  We use the \emph{core CPI} probability question (variable \texttt{PRCCPI}), which asks each forecaster to assign probabilities to 10 ordered bins for fourth-quarter-over-fourth-quarter core CPI inflation in the current and following year.  The 10 bins are (in ascending order): decline, 0.0--0.4\%, 0.5--0.9\%, 1.0--1.4\%, 1.5--1.9\%, 2.0--2.4\%, 2.5--2.9\%, 3.0--3.4\%, 3.5--3.9\%, and $\geq 4.0\%$.

We focus on the 2022:Q1 survey's \emph{next-year} histograms, each forecaster's probability distribution for 2023 core CPI inflation, using the $n=27$ respondents who provided a complete histogram summing to 100\%.  This survey was conducted in February 2022, when realized core CPI was running near 6\% and forecasters disagreed sharply about how quickly inflation would moderate.

\subsection{Aggregate Distributions}

Figure~\ref{fig:spf_heatmap} displays the 27 individual histograms as a heatmap.  The heterogeneity is striking: some forecasters concentrated nearly all mass on 2--3\% (expecting a rapid return to target), while others spread mass across higher bins.  A few placed non-trivial probability on the decline and $\geq 4\%$ tail bins.

\centering

\label{fig:spf_heatmap}

Because the outcomes are ordered, we use the $W_1$ distance (absolute rank difference) as the ground cost.  This respects the ordinal structure of the inflation bins: shifting probability from the 2--3\% bin to the 3--4\% bin incurs less transport cost than shifting it to the deflation bin, correctly reflecting the asymmetry in the outcomes.  By Proposition~\ref{prop:w1}, the $W_1$ barycenter equals the PMF obtained by component-wise CDF median.  We verified this numerically: the closed-form formula and the LP solver agree to machine precision ($\ell^1$ discrepancy $< 10^{-4}$).

Figure~\ref{fig:spf_aggregates} compares the arithmetic mean and the $W_1$ barycenter.  The differences are substantial.  The arithmetic mean places 3.6\% mass on the ``decline'' bin and 8.1\% on the $\geq 4\%$ bin, for a combined tail probability of 11.7\%.  The barycenter assigns \emph{zero} mass to both tails.  Instead, it concentrates 60\% of its mass on the 2.0--2.9\% range and 30\% on 3.0--3.9\%, reflecting the view shared by a majority of forecasters.  The arithmetic mean disperses mass across all bins because a minority of extreme views pull it toward the tails.

\subsection{Historical Forecast Evaluation}
\label{sec:spf_backtest}

\paragraph{Evaluation protocol.}  We use the current-year PRCCPI histograms (columns \texttt{PRCCPI1}--\texttt{PRCCPI10}), which ask each forecaster to distribute probability across the 10 bins for Q4/Q4 core CPI inflation in the current calendar year.  The sample covers 2007:Q1 through 2025:Q4; quarters with fewer than five valid respondents are excluded, leaving 76 quarters.  A respondent's histogram is retained if its probabilities sum to within 2 percentage points of 100\%; retained histograms are renormalized to sum exactly to one and reversed to ascending bin order.  Realized outcomes are Q4/Q4 (December-over-December) percent changes in the seasonally adjusted core CPI from FRED series \texttt{CPILFESL}; the SA and NSA series are nearly identical for this variable.  Each realized value is mapped to one of the 10 bins using the bin boundaries in Table~\ref{tab:backtest}.  We do not assess calibration, which is a property of the individual expert forecasts and is not in general preserved under nonlinear combination; a reliability-diagram analysis is left for future work.

We evaluate each method on two complementary scoring criteria.  The \emph{Ranked Probability Score} (RPS)~\cite{gneiting07} is a strictly proper scoring rule for ordered categorical outcomes defined as $\mathrm{RPS} = \sum_{k=1}^{K-1}(F_{\hat{p}}(k) - \mathbf{1}_{b \leq k})^2$, where $F_{\hat{p}}$ is the aggregate CDF and $b$ is the realized bin; lower RPS indicates better accuracy.  The \emph{probability on the realized bin} (mass score) is the probability mass the aggregate assigns to the bin containing the realized outcome; higher is better.  The two criteria are complementary: the mass score rewards placing mass in precisely the right bin, while RPS additionally penalizes mass placed far from the realized bin.  Significance is assessed via one-sided Wilcoxon signed-rank tests, which are robust to non-normality and heavy tails in score differences---a relevant concern given that both methods assign near-zero mass to the $\geq\!4\%$ bin in most quarters, causing extreme log-score losses in the 2021--2022 inflation episode.

The single-quarter illustration above motivates a question of practical importance: does the $W_1$ barycenter produce better probabilistic forecasts than the arithmetic mean when evaluated against realized outcomes?

\paragraph{Overall results.}  Panel A of Table~\ref{tab:backtest} reports both metrics over the full sample.  The most direct finding is that the barycenter places more mass on the correct bin in 75.0\% of quarters ($p < 0.001$, Wilcoxon), with a mean probability of 0.424 versus 0.367 for the arithmetic mean, a 15.6\% improvement.  The RPS comparison tells a consistent story: the barycenter achieves lower RPS in 68.4\% of quarters ($p = 0.0045$), with mean RPS 0.439 versus 0.447.

\paragraph{Dispersion-conditional results.}  Panel B conditions on the cross-sectional variance of individual forecast means, splitting the sample at the median into low- and high-dispersion quarters (38 each).  The probability-on-realized-bin metric shows the barycenter outperforming in \emph{both} groups: 0.496 versus 0.430 in low-dispersion quarters, and 0.353 versus 0.303 in high-dispersion quarters.  The RPS results are more nuanced: the barycenter wins decisively in low-dispersion quarters (mean RPS 0.301 vs.\ 0.328, $p < 0.001$), while the arithmetic mean is marginally better in high-dispersion quarters (mean RPS 0.567 vs.\ 0.578, $p = 0.37$).  The divergence between the two metrics in high-dispersion quarters reflects a distinction in what they reward.  The barycenter places more mass on the realized bin even in these quarters, but when the realized bin is a tail bin, the AM's broader CDF has smaller cumulative deviations from the realized step function, yielding lower RPS.  The 2021--2022 inflation surge is the clearest illustration: both methods underestimated realized core CPI ($\geq 4\%$), but the AM retained more tail mass, reducing its RPS deficit in those extreme quarters.

In low-dispersion quarters the economic interpretation is straightforward: the panel agrees, the barycenter correctly concentrates mass on the consensus region, and the consensus is usually right.  In high-dispersion quarters the panel is genuinely uncertain; the arithmetic mean's equal weighting preserves more tail mass, which pays off under RPS when an extreme outcome materializes, even though the barycenter still places more mass on the correct bin in expectation.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

This paper proposes the Wasserstein barycenter as a robust method for aggregating expert probability forecasts over finite outcome spaces.  The central theoretical result is Theorem~\ref{thm:l1}: under the indicator ground cost, the barycenter reduces to a constrained $\ell^1$ aggregation problem.  This simplification has two important consequences.  First, it makes computation straightforward: the problem is a convex linear program in $NK$ variables, solvable by standard LP solvers; for ordered spaces, Proposition~\ref{prop:w1} gives a fully closed-form answer (component-wise CDF median) with no solver required.  Second, it identifies the barycenter as a median-type aggregate: the unconstrained minimizer of the $\ell^1$ objective assigns the coordinatewise median to each state, and the simplex constraint shifts this toward a normalized version of the median.  For ordered outcome spaces the closed form is exact: the $W_1$ barycenter is the distribution whose CDF is the component-wise median of the individual CDFs (Proposition~\ref{prop:w1}), a result verified numerically to machine precision in the SPF application.

\paragraph{Robustness interpretation.}  The EIF analysis formalizes the intuition that the barycenter is less sensitive than the arithmetic mean to extreme opinions.  The AM gives every expert equal weight regardless of distance from the consensus; the barycenter penalizes outlying opinions through transport cost, so an expert far from the current aggregate exerts smaller influence.  Empirically, in $90\%$ of simulation replicates the barycenter assigns less probability to an outlier's preferred state than the AM.  The tradeoff is efficiency: under a well-specified homogeneous expert population, the AM has lower MSE by a factor of approximately $1.4$--$1.9$.

\paragraph{Forecast evaluation.}  The historical backtest in Section~\ref{sec:spf_backtest} provides direct evidence that the theoretical properties of the barycenter translate into empirical gains.  The barycenter places more probability mass on the realized outcome bin in 75.0\% of quarters ($p < 0.001$), with a 15.6\% improvement in mean mass on the correct bin (0.424 vs.\ 0.367).  This advantage holds in both low- and high-dispersion quarters.  Under the Ranked Probability Score, the barycenter wins in 68.4\% of quarters overall ($p = 0.0045$), with the advantage concentrated in low-dispersion quarters; the arithmetic mean recovers ground under RPS in high-dispersion quarters because its broader tail coverage reduces cumulative CDF deviations when extreme outcomes materialize.  These results together characterize when each method is preferred: the barycenter is the better aggregator when the panel is broadly in agreement, while the arithmetic mean's equal weighting preserves tail mass that is occasionally valuable under RPS in high-uncertainty periods.  The Wasserstein barycenter is not calibrated by construction---calibration is a property of the individual expert forecasts and is not in general preserved under nonlinear combination.  Comparing the barycenter to alternative methods such as the logarithmic pool and extremization~\cite{satopaa14}, and extending the evaluation to multi-horizon forecasts and other outcome variables, are natural directions for further work.

\paragraph{Applications and extensions.}  The two applications illustrate the method on both unordered (opioid survey, indicator distance) and ordered (SPF, $W_1$ distance) outcome spaces.  A natural extension is to weighted barycenters, in which experts receive unequal weights reflecting their track record or domain expertise.  A natural population model for expert probability vectors is the Dirichlet distribution; however, aggregation via Dirichlet MLE does not generally satisfy boundedness or unanimity, and therefore differs conceptually from the deterministic aggregation rules studied here.  The extension to probability distributions over network-valued outcomes (Appendix~\ref{app:networks}) illustrates that the framework applies to any finite metric space without modification.  The multivariate case, in which experts provide joint distributions over multiple outcomes, raises new questions about the structure of the barycenter that we leave for future work.

\section{Barycenters for Network-Valued Outcomes}
\label{app:networks}

The framework extends naturally to settings where the outcome space is a set of network structures.  Let $\mathcal{Y}_n$ denote the space of binary adjacency matrices on $n$ nodes: the set of $n\times n$ zero-diagonal matrices with entries in $\left\{0,1\right\}$, equipped with the Hamming distance
\begin{align*}
d(Y,Y')=\sum_{m\neq n}\left|Y_{mn}-Y'_{mn}\right|.
\end{align*}
A probability mass function over $\mathcal{Y}_n$ assigns probability $p_j(Y)$ to each network $Y\in\mathcal{Y}_n$.  Existence of the barycenter follows from the compactness argument of Theorem~\ref{thm:l1} applied to the finite metric space $(\mathcal{Y}_n, d)$.  The full-diagonal criterion (Proposition~\ref{prop:fulldiag}) carries over: in any optimal solution, $\Pi^{(j)}_{YY}=\min\{p_j(Y),\Psi(Y)\}$ for each $Y$.

Unlike the unordered case where all off-diagonal pairs are equidistant, pairs of networks differing in $k$ edges incur cost $k$ under $W_1$ (or $k^2$ under $W_2$).  After diagonal entries are maximized, remaining mass is assigned to off-diagonal entries in order of increasing Hamming distance.

The result extends to integer-valued (weighted) networks by replacing $\{0,1\}$ with $\{0,\ldots,M\}$ and using the $\ell^1$ Hamming distance.

\section{Full-Diagonal Criterion}
\label{app:fulldiag}

The following proposition extends Lemma~\ref{lem:ot_diagonal} from individual transport plans to the full barycenter solution: in any optimal solution, every transport plan simultaneously maximises its diagonal.

\begin{proposition}[Full-Diagonal Criterion]
\label{prop:fulldiag}
Let $\Pi^{1},\ldots,\Pi^{N}$ be $K\times K$ matrices representing the joint distributions between a candidate barycenter $\Psi$ and the probability mass functions $p_1,\ldots,p_N$ on $\Omega=\left\{1,\ldots,K\right\}$.  Suppose the matrices are feasible: for all $l$ and $k$,
\begin{align*}
\sum_{j}\Pi_{k,j}^{l}=p_l(k),\qquad
\sum_{j}\Pi_{j,k}^{l}=\Psi(k),
\end{align*}
but for some $l$ and $i$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Pi_ineq}
\Pi^{l}_{i,i} < \min\bigl\{p_l(i),\,\Psi(i)\bigr\}.
\end{equation}
Then there exists a feasible $\tilde{\Pi}^{l}\neq \Pi^{l}$ such that
$f(\tilde{\Pi}^l,\Pi^{-l}) < f(\Pi^l,\Pi^{-l})$,
so $(\Pi^1,\ldots,\Pi^N)$ is not optimal.  Consequently, every optimal transport plan satisfies $\Pi^{l}_{kk}=\min\{p_l(k),\Psi(k)\}$ for all $k$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
The objective equals $f(\mathbf{\Pi})= \sum_{l=1}^N \sum_{k=1}^K (1 - \Pi_{k,k}^{l})$, so increasing any diagonal entry strictly decreases the objective.  Suppose \eqref{eq:Pi_ineq} holds.  Since $\Pi^l_{ii} < p_l(i)$, there exists $k_1\neq i$ with $\Pi^l_{i,k_1}>0$.  Setting $\tilde\Pi^l_{i,i}=\Pi^l_{i,i}+\Pi^l_{i,k_1}$ and $\tilde\Pi^l_{i,k_1}=0$ preserves row $i$'s sum.  Since $\Pi^l_{ii}<\Psi(i)$, the column-$i$ sum $\Psi(i)$ exceeds the diagonal; there exists a set of indices $\{k^i_2\}$ and weights $\{\epsilon_i\}$ summing to $\Pi^l_{i,k_1}$ such that transferring mass $\epsilon_i\Pi^l_{k^i_2,i}$ from column $i$ to column $k_1$ restores all column sums.  The resulting $\tilde\Pi^l$ is feasible and the objective decreases by $\Pi^l_{i,k_1}>0$.
\end{proof}
